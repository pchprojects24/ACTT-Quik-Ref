Adult Bradycardia with a Pulse Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Heart rate typically < 50/min that is inadequate for the patient's clinical condition (AHA ACLS 2020, reaffirmed 2024 focused update).

Primary Assessment

Identify and treat underlying causes. Maintain patent airway, assist breathing as necessary, and administer oxygen only if hypoxemic (titrate SpO2 to 94 to 99%). Attach cardiac monitor, monitor blood pressure and oximetry. Establish IV/IO access and obtain a 12-lead ECG when available (do not delay therapy).

Assess for Hemodynamic Instability

Look for any of: hypotension, acutely altered mental status, signs of shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure.

  • Stable / asymptomatic: Monitor, identify reversible causes, obtain 12-lead ECG, consider expert consultation.
  • Unstable / symptomatic:
    • First-line: Atropine 1 mg IV bolus. May repeat every 3 to 5 minutes to a maximum total dose of 3 mg.

      Caution: Atropine is unlikely to be effective in second-degree Mobitz II or third-degree AV block, or in transplanted (denervated) hearts — do not delay pacing or chronotropic infusions in these settings.

    • If Atropine is ineffective or unlikely to work, use one of the following without delay:
      • Transcutaneous pacing (TCP). Confirm electrical and mechanical capture; provide analgesia/sedation (e.g., Fentanyl and/or Midazolam) for conscious patients.
      • Dopamine infusion 5 to 20 mcg/kg/min IV, titrated to patient response.
      • Epinephrine infusion 2 to 10 mcg/min IV, titrated to patient response.
    • Consider expert consultation and transvenous pacing if symptomatic bradycardia persists.

Identify and Treat Underlying Cause

  • Acute coronary ischemia / inferior MI.
  • Hypoxia — ensure airway and oxygenation.
  • Electrolyte abnormalities (hyperkalemia — consider Calcium gluconate or Calcium chloride).
  • Drug effects: beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, opioids (consider Naloxone), organophosphates.
  • Increased vagal tone, hypothermia, or raised intracranial pressure.
Adult Tachycardia with a Pulse Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Sustained tachyarrhythmia, generally HR ≥ 150/min, in a patient with a pulse (AHA ACLS 2020, current).

Primary Assessment

Maintain patent airway, assist breathing as needed, administer oxygen only if hypoxemic (target SpO2 ≥ 94%). Attach cardiac monitor to identify rhythm, monitor blood pressure and oximetry. Establish IV access.

Assess for Hemodynamic Instability

Look for any of: hypotension, acutely altered mental status, signs of shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure that is caused by the arrhythmia.

  • Unstable — Synchronized Cardioversion:
    • Provide procedural sedation if conscious and time permits (e.g., Ketamine 1 mg/kg IV over 60 seconds, Midazolam 1 to 2.5 mg IV, or Etomidate 0.1 to 0.3 mg/kg IV). Add an analgesic (e.g., Fentanyl) when appropriate.
    • Deliver synchronized cardioversion at the rhythm-appropriate biphasic energy (see Electrical Cardioversion Algorithm).
    • If regular narrow complex (likely SVT), Adenosine 6 mg IV rapid push may be considered while preparing cardioversion — do not delay cardioversion.
  • Stable:
    • Establish IV access and obtain a 12-lead ECG.
    • Wide QRS (≥ 0.12 sec):
      • If regular and monomorphic, consider Adenosine (diagnostic and may convert SVT with aberrancy): 6 mg IV rapid push, then 12 mg if needed. Do not use adenosine for irregular or polymorphic wide-complex tachycardia.
      • Antiarrhythmic infusion (any one):
        • Procainamide 20 to 50 mg/min IV (max 17 mg/kg) until arrhythmia suppressed, hypotension occurs, QRS widens by > 50%, or max dose given. Maintenance 1 to 4 mg/min. Avoid if prolonged QT or CHF.
        • Amiodarone 150 mg IV over 10 minutes; may repeat as needed for recurrent VT (max 2.2 g/24 h). Maintenance 1 mg/min for 6 hours, then 0.5 mg/min.
        • Sotalol 100 mg (1.5 mg/kg) IV over 5 minutes (avoid if prolonged QT).
      • For polymorphic VT with prolonged QT (Torsades de Pointes): Magnesium sulfate 1 to 2 g IV over 15 minutes; correct electrolytes; consider overdrive pacing.
      • Consult expert; if rhythm becomes unstable, proceed to synchronized cardioversion.
    • Narrow QRS (< 0.12 sec):
      • Vagal maneuvers (Valsalva, including modified/REVERT technique; carotid sinus massage if no contraindications).
      • Adenosine if regular: 6 mg IV rapid push followed by 20 mL saline flush; if no conversion, 12 mg IV rapid push (a second 12 mg dose may be given).
      • If irregular (likely atrial fibrillation/flutter or MAT): rate control with Diltiazem, Verapamil, or Metoprolol (avoid AV-nodal blockers in pre-excited AF/WPW).
      • Consult expert.

Identify and Treat Underlying Cause

ACS, electrolyte/acid-base disturbance, hypovolemia, hypoxia, fever/sepsis, drug toxicity (sympathomimetics, anticholinergics), hyperthyroidism, pulmonary embolism.

Electrical Cardioversion Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Tachyarrhythmia with a pulse that is causing serious signs or symptoms of hemodynamic instability (hypotension, acutely altered mental status, shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure) due to the arrhythmia (AHA ACLS 2020).

Preparation

  1. Verify IV access, suction, BVM, and airway equipment immediately available; pre-oxygenate.
  2. Continuous ECG, SpO2, BP, capnography, and defibrillator pads in anterior-lateral or anterior-posterior position.
  3. Administer procedural sedation if the patient is conscious and time permits:
    • Ketamine 1 mg/kg IV push over 60 seconds, or
    • Midazolam 1 to 2.5 mg IV direct (titrate), or
    • Etomidate 0.1 to 0.3 mg/kg IV, or
    • Propofol 0.5 to 1 mg/kg IV (caution with hypotension).
    Consider an analgesic such as Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg IV.
  4. Turn on defibrillator and press the SYNC button. Visually confirm sync markers appear on each R wave (re-check sync after every shock — some devices revert to unsynchronized).

Energy Selection (Biphasic)

  • Narrow regular (SVT, Atrial Flutter): start 50 to 100 J.
  • Narrow irregular (Atrial Fibrillation): start 120 to 200 J.
  • Wide regular (Monomorphic VT with pulse): start 100 J.
  • Wide irregular (Polymorphic VT): treat as VF — deliver unsynchronized high-energy defibrillation (120 to 200 J biphasic).
  • If the first shock fails, escalate energy in a stepwise fashion per device manufacturer recommendations.

Execution

  1. State "Charging." Announce "Clear — I'm clear, you're clear, oxygen clear" and ensure no contact with patient, bed, or O2 source.
  2. Press and hold the SHOCK button until shock is delivered.
  3. Immediately reassess rhythm and pulse; if VF/pulseless VT, begin CPR and follow Cardiac Arrest Algorithm.
  4. If unstable rhythm persists, re-sync and re-shock at increased energy.

Pitfalls: failure to engage SYNC, sync inadvertently capturing T waves in polymorphic VT (use unsynchronized shock), inadequate sedation, and forgetting to disconnect oxygen flow over the chest before shocking.

Adult BLS Algorithm for Healthcare Providers

Clinical Indication

Suspected cardiac arrest in an adult (AHA BLS for Healthcare Providers, 2020 Guidelines and 2023 focused update).

Initial Steps

  1. Verify scene safety; don PPE.
  2. Check for responsiveness (tap and shout). If unresponsive, shout for nearby help, activate the emergency response system (or send second rescuer), and obtain an AED/defibrillator.
  3. Simultaneously check for breathing (look for no breathing or only gasping) and palpate the carotid pulse for no more than 10 seconds.

Action Matrix

  • Normal breathing, pulse present: Place in recovery position if no trauma; monitor; reassess until EMS arrives.
  • No normal breathing, pulse present (respiratory arrest): Provide rescue breathing 1 breath every 6 seconds (10/min); each breath over 1 second producing visible chest rise. Recheck pulse every 2 minutes. If suspected opioid overdose, administer Naloxone per local protocol.
  • No breathing or only gasping, no pulse: Begin CPR immediately, starting with chest compressions. Use AED as soon as it arrives.

High-Quality CPR (every cycle)

  • Compression rate 100 to 120/min.
  • Compression depth 2 to 2.4 inches (5 to 6 cm) in adults; allow full chest recoil after each compression.
  • Minimize interruptions in compressions (target chest compression fraction > 60%, ideally > 80%).
  • Compression-to-ventilation ratio 30:2 without an advanced airway (single or two-rescuer adult).
  • With an advanced airway: continuous compressions at 100 to 120/min and 1 breath every 6 seconds (10/min). Avoid hyperventilation.
  • Rotate compressors every 2 minutes (or sooner if fatigued) to maintain quality.
  • Use real-time CPR feedback (audiovisual device or quantitative waveform capnography) when available; PETCO2 < 10 mmHg suggests poor CPR quality.

AED Use

  • Power on the AED, attach pads, follow voice prompts, minimize interruption to compressions while pads are placed.
  • Shockable rhythm (VF/pulseless VT): Clear the patient, deliver 1 shock, then resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes before next rhythm check.
  • Non-shockable rhythm: Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes, then re-analyze.
  • Continue cycles until ALS providers take over, the patient shows signs of life, or the rescuer is exhausted.

Special Considerations

  • Suspected opioid overdose with a pulse but inadequate breathing: provide ventilations and administer Naloxone.
  • Pregnancy: perform manual left-uterine displacement during CPR; standard BLS otherwise.
  • Hypothermia: continue resuscitation while rewarming — "not dead until warm and dead."
Adult Cardiac Arrest Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Confirmed cardiac arrest in an adult (unresponsive, no normal breathing, no pulse). Per AHA ACLS 2020 Guidelines (with 2023 and 2024 focused updates).

Continuous Actions

  • High-quality CPR: rate 100 to 120/min, depth 2 to 2.4 inches (5 to 6 cm), full recoil, chest compression fraction > 60%, minimize interruptions.
  • Provide oxygen during CPR; attach cardiac monitor / defibrillator pads as soon as available.
  • Establish IV access (preferred) or IO access if IV cannot be rapidly obtained.
  • Switch compressors every 2 minutes (or sooner if fatigued).
  • Use quantitative waveform capnography to monitor CPR quality and detect ROSC (PETCO2 < 10 mmHg suggests poor compressions; abrupt sustained rise often signals ROSC).
  • If advanced airway: 1 breath every 6 seconds (10/min) with continuous compressions; avoid hyperventilation.

Shockable Rhythm (VF / Pulseless VT)

  1. Shock 120 to 200 J biphasic (or 360 J monophasic); use device-specific energy when known. Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes.
  2. Rhythm check — if still shockable: Shock, then resume CPR for 2 minutes. Establish IV/IO access if not already done. Consider advanced airway and waveform capnography.
  3. Administer Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO every 3 to 5 minutes (give after the second shock for VF/pVT).
  4. Rhythm check — if still shockable: Shock, then resume CPR for 2 minutes. Administer an antiarrhythmic:
    • Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO bolus first dose; second dose 150 mg IV/IO, OR
    • Lidocaine 1 to 1.5 mg/kg IV/IO first dose; second dose 0.5 to 0.75 mg/kg IV/IO.
  5. For Torsades de Pointes / suspected hypomagnesemia: Magnesium sulfate 1 to 2 g IV/IO diluted in 10 mL D5W.
  6. Continue 2-minute CPR cycles, rhythm checks, shocks, and Epinephrine every 3 to 5 minutes. Treat reversible causes throughout.

Non-Shockable Rhythm (Asystole / PEA)

  1. Resume CPR for 2 minutes; establish IV/IO access; consider advanced airway and waveform capnography.
  2. Administer Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO as soon as feasible, then every 3 to 5 minutes (early epinephrine emphasized in 2020 update).
  3. Rhythm check every 2 minutes; if rhythm becomes shockable, switch to the VF/pVT pathway.
  4. Aggressively search for and treat reversible causes (H's and T's).

Reversible Causes (H's and T's)

  • Hypovolemia — IV/IO crystalloid bolus.
  • Hypoxia — airway, oxygen, ventilation.
  • Hydrogen ion (acidosis) — ventilation; consider Sodium bicarbonate for known severe acidosis, hyperkalemia, or TCA overdose.
  • Hypo-/Hyperkalemia — replace K+ if low (KCl); for hyperkalemia give Calcium chloride or Calcium gluconate, Insulin with Dextrose, Sodium bicarbonate.
  • Hypothermia — active rewarming; continue prolonged resuscitation.
  • Tension pneumothorax — needle decompression / finger thoracostomy.
  • Tamponade (cardiac) — pericardiocentesis.
  • Toxins — antidotes (Naloxone for opioids, Glucagon or calcium for β-blocker / CCB overdose, Sodium bicarbonate for TCA, lipid emulsion for local anaesthetic systemic toxicity).
  • Thrombosis (pulmonary) — consider fibrinolysis.
  • Thrombosis (coronary) — emergent PCI after ROSC.

Termination considerations: Use end-tidal CO2 trends, downtime, age, witnessed status, initial rhythm, response to ACLS, and patient wishes; involve team and base-hospital physician per local protocol.

Adult Post-Cardiac Arrest Rhythm Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Return of Spontaneous Circulation (ROSC) after adult cardiac arrest. Aligned with the Heart & Stroke Foundation of Canada / AHA 2020 Guidelines for CPR and ECC, 2023 and 2024 focused updates on ACLS and post-arrest care.

Immediate Post-ROSC Priorities

  • Confirm ROSC (palpable pulse, sustained rise in PETCO2, arterial waveform).
  • Reassess ABCDE; do not interrupt ongoing CPR chest compressions until ROSC is certain.
  • Continue quantitative waveform capnography and cardiac monitoring; establish a second large-bore IV if not already in place.

Management Steps

  1. Airway and Breathing:
    • Place an advanced airway (ETT or supraglottic) if not already in situ; confirm with waveform capnography.
    • Start mechanical ventilation at 10 breaths/min; titrate to PETCO2 35–40 mmHg and PaCO2 35–45 mmHg. Avoid hyperventilation and hypocapnia (causes cerebral vasoconstriction).
    • Titrate FiO2 to the lowest level that achieves SpO2 92–98% (or PaO2 > 80 mmHg). Avoid hyperoxia and hypoxia.
    • Use low tidal volume lung-protective ventilation (6–8 mL/kg ideal body weight); elevate head of bed to 30° if not contraindicated.
  2. Hemodynamics: Treat hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg or MAP < 65 mmHg).
    • IV/IO isotonic crystalloid bolus 1–2 L (use smaller aliquots if cardiogenic shock or pulmonary edema is suspected).
    • Initiate vasopressor infusion: Norepinephrine 0.1–0.5 mcg/kg/min (typical start 2–4 mcg/min) titrated to MAP ≥ 65 mmHg; alternatives include Epinephrine 2–10 mcg/min or Dopamine 5–10 mcg/kg/min.
    • Consider Epinephrine 5–20 mcg IV push-dose for transient severe hypotension while preparing infusion.
  3. Diagnostics: Obtain a 12-lead ECG; perform bedside POCUS (cardiac, lung, IVC) if available; draw arterial blood gas, lactate, electrolytes, glucose, troponin, and coagulation panel.
  4. Coronary Reperfusion: If STEMI is present, ongoing ischemia is suspected, or the arrest was of presumed cardiac origin with unstable hemodynamics or cardiogenic shock, activate emergent coronary angiography / PCI and transport to a PCI-capable centre. For selected comatose patients without STEMI but with shockable initial rhythm, discuss urgent angiography with the interventional team.
  5. Targeted Temperature Management (Temperature Control): For patients who remain comatose (unable to follow commands) after ROSC, select and maintain a constant target temperature between 32°C and 37.5°C for at least 24 hours (2023/2024 Heart & Stroke / AHA focused update). After the initial 24 hours, actively prevent fever (< 37.7°C) for at least 72 hours. Use surface or intravascular cooling devices with continuous core temperature monitoring.
  6. Glycemic Control: Monitor blood glucose; treat hypoglycemia with Dextrose and treat hyperglycemia per local protocol (target glucose ~7.8–10.0 mmol/L). Avoid hypoglycemia.
  7. Neurological Care: Obtain non-contrast head CT (rule out intracranial pathology as arrest cause). Initiate continuous or frequent intermittent EEG monitoring in comatose patients. Treat documented seizures (e.g., Lorazepam, Midazolam, then Phenytoin or Valproic acid). Routine seizure prophylaxis is not recommended.
  8. Sedation and Analgesia: Titrate short-acting agents (e.g., Fentanyl, Propofol, or Midazolam) to patient comfort and ventilator synchrony; minimize deep sedation that obscures neurological assessment.
  9. Identify and Treat Reversible Causes: Re-address the H's and T's; repeat focused ultrasound as needed.
  10. Disposition and Prognostication: Transport to an ICU capable of post-arrest care, cardiac catheterization, and neuroprognostication. Defer formal neuroprognostication for at least 72 hours after ROSC (or 72 hours after return to normothermia in patients receiving temperature control).

Pitfalls: hyperventilation causing cerebral vasoconstriction, hyperoxia (re-perfusion injury), unrecognized re-arrest, premature withdrawal of life-sustaining therapies before adequate neuroprognostication, and failure to prevent fever in the 24–72 h window.

Management of Acute Cardiac Chest Pain

Clinical Indication

Adult patient with symptoms suggestive of acute coronary syndrome (ACS): chest discomfort (pressure, squeezing, heaviness), referred pain (jaw, arm, epigastrium), dyspnea, diaphoresis, nausea/vomiting, or anginal equivalents — especially in older adults, women, and patients with diabetes. Aligned with Canadian Cardiovascular Society / Heart & Stroke Foundation and AHA ACS guidelines (2020, 2023 focused update).

Algorithm Steps

  1. Primary Survey: Assess ABCs; ensure patent airway, continuous cardiac monitoring, SpO2, blood pressure (both arms), and IV access. Perform a focused history (OPQRST, cardiac risk factors, prior MI/PCI/CABG, medications including sildenafil/tadalafil/vardenafil in the prior 24–48 h).
  2. Oxygen: Administer supplemental oxygen only if SpO2 < 90% or the patient is in respiratory distress (routine oxygen in non-hypoxemic ACS is not beneficial and may be harmful).
  3. 12-Lead ECG: Obtain within 10 minutes of first medical contact; repeat every 15–30 minutes if initial ECG is non-diagnostic but symptoms persist. Consider right-sided (V4R) and posterior leads (V7–V9) when inferior STEMI or posterior ischemia is suspected.
  4. Aspirin (ASA): 160 to 325 mg non-enteric-coated, chewed and swallowed (unless true allergy or active GI bleeding).
  5. Nitrates: Nitroglycerin 0.4 mg sublingual spray or tablet every 5 minutes PRN for ischemic symptoms (maximum 3 doses). Contraindicated if SBP < 90 mmHg or drop > 30 mmHg from baseline, HR < 50 or > 100 bpm, suspected right ventricular infarction (inferior STEMI with RV involvement), or phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor use within 24 h (48 h for tadalafil). Transition to IV Nitroglycerin infusion (start 10 mcg/min, titrate by 10 mcg/min q5min to max 200 mcg/min) for ongoing ischemia, hypertension, or heart failure.
  6. Analgesia: Morphine 1 to 5 mg IV every 5 to 15 minutes PRN for ongoing ischemic pain unresponsive to nitrates (use judiciously — morphine may delay P2Y12 absorption and is associated with worse outcomes in some ACS cohorts). Fentanyl 25–50 mcg IV is a reasonable alternative.
  7. Antiemetic: Dimenhydrinate 25 to 50 mg IV or Ondansetron 4 mg IV for nausea/vomiting.
  8. Classify ECG and Determine Reperfusion Strategy:
    • STEMI (ST elevation ≥ 1 mm in ≥ 2 contiguous limb leads, ≥ 2 mm in V2–V3 for men ≥ 40, ≥ 2.5 mm for men < 40, ≥ 1.5 mm for women; or new LBBB with clinical suspicion): proceed to STEMI Management / Fibrinolytic pathway. Target first-medical-contact-to-device ≤ 90 min (PCI) or door-to-needle ≤ 30 min (fibrinolysis).
    • NSTEMI / Unstable Angina (ST depression, T wave inversion, or normal ECG with positive troponin): initiate antiplatelet + anticoagulant therapy; risk-stratify with HEART, GRACE, or TIMI scores; arrange timely (within 24–72 h) invasive strategy based on risk.
    • Non-cardiac etiology: consider aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, pericarditis, esophageal causes, musculoskeletal pain; investigate with appropriate imaging and labs.
  9. Antiplatelet — P2Y12 Inhibitor (per local protocol and reperfusion strategy): Clopidogrel 300 mg PO (600 mg if primary PCI; 75 mg if age ≥ 75 and receiving fibrinolysis), Ticagrelor 180 mg PO, or Prasugrel 60 mg PO (PCI only, contraindicated with prior stroke/TIA and avoided if age ≥ 75 or weight < 60 kg).
  10. Anticoagulation: Unfractionated Heparin 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4000 units) then 12 units/kg/h infusion (max 1000 units/h), OR Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC q12h (30 mg IV bolus may be given with fibrinolysis in patients < 75 years) per local protocol.
  11. Beta-blocker: Oral beta-blocker within the first 24 hours in stable ACS without contraindications (hypotension, bradycardia, heart failure, high-degree AV block, active bronchospasm); IV Metoprolol is reserved for specific indications (e.g., persistent hypertension or ongoing ischemia) and not routine.
  12. Continuous Monitoring and Serial Assessment: Repeat ECGs, monitor for arrhythmia, serial troponin (high-sensitivity troponin at 0 and 1–3 h), reassess pain, vitals, and mental status. Treat arrhythmias per relevant ACLS algorithm.

Inferior STEMI / RV infarction pearl: hypotension after Nitroglycerin suggests RV involvement — stop nitrates, give IV crystalloid bolus, obtain V4R.

Fibrinolytic Checklist

Clinical Indication

Screening tool for STEMI patients being considered for fibrinolytic (thrombolytic) therapy. Use when primary PCI cannot be delivered within 120 minutes of first medical contact and symptom onset is ≤ 12 hours (may extend to 24 hours with ongoing symptoms or hemodynamic/electrical instability). Based on Canadian Cardiovascular Society / Heart & Stroke Foundation and AHA STEMI guidelines (2013, 2017, updated 2022/2024). Confirm all YES inclusion criteria and verify the absence of every absolute contraindication before administration.

Inclusion Criteria (all must be YES)

  • Ischemic chest discomfort ≥ 20 minutes and ≤ 12 hours duration.
  • 12-lead ECG criteria for STEMI: ST elevation ≥ 1 mm in ≥ 2 contiguous limb leads or ≥ 2 mm in V2–V3 (≥ 1.5 mm in women), new or presumed new LBBB, or true posterior MI (ST depression V1–V3 with upright T waves and ST elevation in V7–V9).
  • Primary PCI is not available within 120 minutes of first medical contact.

Absolute Contraindications (ANY YES = do not give fibrinolytic)

  • Any prior intracranial hemorrhage.
  • Known structural cerebral vascular lesion (e.g., arteriovenous malformation, aneurysm).
  • Known malignant intracranial neoplasm (primary or metastatic).
  • Ischemic stroke within 3 months (EXCEPT acute ischemic stroke within 4.5 hours being treated with fibrinolysis).
  • Suspected aortic dissection.
  • Active bleeding or bleeding diathesis (excluding menses).
  • Significant closed-head or facial trauma within 3 months.
  • Intracranial or intraspinal surgery within 2 months.
  • Severe uncontrolled hypertension unresponsive to emergency therapy (SBP > 180 or DBP > 110 mmHg despite treatment).
  • For streptokinase: prior streptokinase/anistreplase exposure within the last 6 months (not applicable to Tenecteplase).

Relative Contraindications (require clinical judgment — weigh risk vs. benefit)

  • History of chronic, severe, poorly controlled hypertension.
  • Severe uncontrolled hypertension on presentation (SBP > 180 or DBP > 110 mmHg) — may be treated with IV Labetalol or Nitroglycerin infusion before fibrinolysis.
  • History of prior ischemic stroke > 3 months, dementia, or known intracranial pathology not listed above.
  • Traumatic or prolonged (> 10 minutes) CPR.
  • Major surgery within 3 weeks.
  • Recent internal bleeding (within 2 to 4 weeks).
  • Noncompressible vascular punctures.
  • Active peptic ulcer disease.
  • Pregnancy or < 1 week postpartum.
  • Current oral anticoagulant use (higher INR = higher bleeding risk).
  • Advanced age (≥ 75 years): increased intracranial hemorrhage risk — consider half-dose Tenecteplase where supported by local protocol (STREAM/STREAM-2 evidence).

Required Measurements / Pre-Administration Checks

  • Bilateral blood pressures and heart rate.
  • Two patent IVs (one reserved for fibrinolytic infusion; flush dextrose-containing lines with NS before/after TNK).
  • Baseline labs where available: CBC, INR/PTT, electrolytes, creatinine, troponin, type and screen.
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring and defibrillator at the bedside (reperfusion arrhythmias are common).
  • Confirm door-to-needle target ≤ 30 minutes.

Pharmacoinvasive strategy (Canadian standard): after successful fibrinolysis, transfer promptly to a PCI-capable centre for angiography within 2–24 hours; proceed to rescue PCI urgently if fibrinolysis fails (persistent ST elevation with < 50% resolution at 60–90 min, hemodynamic/electrical instability, or ongoing ischemia).

Regional STEMI Fibrinolytic Therapy Orders

Clinical Indication

Pre-printed order set for adult STEMI patients receiving fibrinolysis in the Regional STEMI Program. Confirm inclusion criteria and absence of absolute contraindications (see Fibrinolytic Checklist) and obtain consent (informed or deferred) where possible. Target door-to-needle time ≤ 30 minutes.

Pre-Fibrinolytic Orders

  • Cardiac monitor with defibrillator at bedside; continuous SpO2 and NIBP q5min × 4 then q15min.
  • Two patent peripheral IVs (18 gauge preferred); avoid non-compressible vascular access, IM injections, and arterial punctures.
  • Serial 12-lead ECGs pre-fibrinolysis, at 60 and 90 minutes, and PRN for symptom change or arrhythmia.
  • Bloodwork: CBC, electrolytes, creatinine, glucose, INR/PTT, troponin, lipase, type and screen.
  • Oxygen to maintain SpO2 ≥ 90% (do not give routine oxygen if SpO2 ≥ 90%).

Antiplatelet and Anticoagulant Orders

  • Aspirin (ASA): 160 to 325 mg chewed and swallowed (non-enteric-coated), then 81 mg PO daily.
  • P2Y12 Inhibitor (per program formulary): Clopidogrel 300 mg PO loading dose if < 75 years (75 mg if ≥ 75 years with fibrinolysis), then 75 mg PO daily.
  • Anticoagulant (one of):
    • Enoxaparin: if age < 75 years, 30 mg IV bolus followed by 1 mg/kg SC q12h (max 100 mg for first two SC doses); if age ≥ 75 years, no IV bolus, 0.75 mg/kg SC q12h (max 75 mg for first two SC doses); reduce to once daily if CrCl < 30 mL/min.
    • Unfractionated Heparin: 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4000 units) followed by 12 units/kg/h infusion (max 1000 units/h); titrate to aPTT 1.5–2.0 × control for 48 h.

Fibrinolytic Order

  • Tenecteplase (TNKase): single IV bolus over 5 seconds; dose weight-based (see Tenecteplase Instructions). Consider half-dose TNK for patients ≥ 75 years per local protocol to reduce intracranial hemorrhage risk.

Symptom Control

  • Nitroglycerin: 0.4 mg sublingual spray or tablet q5min PRN for ischemic symptoms, maximum 3 doses. If not resolved after 3 doses, transition to IV infusion. Hold if SBP < 90 mmHg (or drop > 30 mmHg from baseline), HR < 50 or > 100, suspected RV infarction, or recent phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor use.
  • Nitroglycerin IV Infusion: start at 10 mcg/min; titrate by 10 mcg/min q5min PRN (maximum rate 200 mcg/min). Maintain SBP > 90 mmHg (or no strict drop > 30 mmHg from baseline).
  • Morphine: 1 to 5 mg IV q5–15min PRN for ongoing ischemic symptoms unresponsive to nitrates (use cautiously — may reduce P2Y12 absorption).
  • Dimenhydrinate: 25 to 50 mg IV or PO q4h PRN for nausea/vomiting.
  • Acetaminophen: 325 to 650 mg PO q4h PRN for non-ischemic pain (maximum 4 g / 24 h; 3 g / 24 h if hepatic impairment or malnourished).
  • Lorazepam: 1 mg PO or sublingual q4h PRN for anxiety or restlessness.

Electrolyte and Arrhythmia Management

  • Contact medical director for Potassium chloride supplementation if K+ < 3.5 mmol/L (target 4.0 mmol/L in ACS).
  • Maintain Mg2+ > 0.8 mmol/L; give Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 15 minutes PRN for documented hypomagnesemia, torsades de pointes, or refractory VT/VF.
  • Ventricular arrhythmia following reperfusion: treat per ACLS algorithm (defibrillation for pulseless VT/VF; Amiodarone 150 mg IV over 10 min or Lidocaine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV for stable VT).
  • Symptomatic bradycardia / AV block (common with inferior MI): see Bradycardia algorithm — atropine, transcutaneous pacing, or Dopamine / Epinephrine infusion.

Monitoring and Transfer

  • Vital signs and neuro checks q5min during TNK bolus and for 15 min; then q15min × 1 h; then q30min × 2 h; then q1h.
  • Reassess ECG at 60 and 90 minutes. Failed fibrinolysis = < 50% resolution of ST elevation in the worst lead at 60–90 min, ongoing ischemia, hemodynamic/electrical instability → activate rescue PCI.
  • Transfer to PCI-capable centre for pharmacoinvasive angiography within 2–24 hours of successful fibrinolysis.
  • Monitor for bleeding (especially intracranial): any new neurologic change → stop anticoagulants, CT head stat.
ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction Management

Clinical Indication

Adult patient with ischemic symptoms and 12-lead ECG consistent with ST-elevation myocardial infarction. Aligned with Canadian Cardiovascular Society / Heart & Stroke Foundation STEMI guidelines (2019, 2022/2024 updates) and AHA/ACC STEMI guidelines.

Diagnosis — STEMI ECG Criteria

  • ST elevation at the J point in ≥ 2 contiguous leads: ≥ 1 mm in limb leads; ≥ 2 mm in V2–V3 for men ≥ 40 years; ≥ 2.5 mm for men < 40 years; ≥ 1.5 mm for women.
  • New or presumed new left bundle branch block with clinical suspicion of ischemia (Sgarbossa criteria may assist).
  • True posterior MI: tall R in V1–V2 with horizontal ST depression; confirm with V7–V9 showing ST elevation ≥ 0.5 mm.
  • de Winter T-waves, hyperacute T-waves, and Wellens pattern are STEMI equivalents — consult cardiology.

Initial Management (within 10 minutes of first medical contact)

  • Perform 12-lead ECG; activate the STEMI pathway.
  • Aspirin (ASA) 160–325 mg chewed.
  • Oxygen only if SpO2 < 90% or respiratory distress.
  • Nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL q5min PRN x 3 doses (observe contraindications).
  • Morphine 1–5 mg IV q5–15min PRN for refractory ischemic pain; Fentanyl is an alternative.
  • Two large-bore IVs; continuous cardiac monitoring with defibrillator at bedside.

Reperfusion Strategy Selection

  • Primary PCI (preferred): if expected time from first medical contact to device deployment is ≤ 120 minutes (target ≤ 90 minutes for direct presentation, ≤ 120 minutes for transferred patients). Door-in-door-out at non-PCI centres ≤ 30 minutes.
  • Fibrinolysis: if PCI cannot be delivered within 120 minutes and symptom onset is ≤ 12 hours (may be considered up to 24 hours with ongoing ischemia, hemodynamic or electrical instability). Target door-to-needle ≤ 30 minutes. Screen with Fibrinolytic Checklist.
  • Cardiogenic shock or severe heart failure: emergent PCI regardless of time from onset (fibrinolysis is less effective in shock).

Adjunctive Antiplatelet Therapy

  • ASA: 160–325 mg loading, then 81 mg PO daily indefinitely.
  • P2Y12 inhibitor (dual antiplatelet therapy, DAPT):
    • Primary PCI: Ticagrelor 180 mg PO loading then 90 mg BID, OR Prasugrel 60 mg PO loading then 10 mg daily (contraindicated with prior stroke/TIA, caution if age ≥ 75 or weight < 60 kg), OR Clopidogrel 600 mg PO loading then 75 mg daily (if ticagrelor/prasugrel unavailable or contraindicated).
    • Fibrinolysis: Clopidogrel 300 mg PO loading (if age < 75) or 75 mg (if age ≥ 75), then 75 mg daily. Ticagrelor may be substituted after initial clopidogrel loading per local protocol.

Adjunctive Anticoagulation

  • With Primary PCI: Unfractionated Heparin 70–100 units/kg IV bolus (50–70 units/kg if GPIIb/IIIa inhibitor used), titrated to ACT. Bivalirudin is an alternative where available.
  • With Fibrinolysis:
    • Enoxaparin preferred: age < 75 years — 30 mg IV bolus then 1 mg/kg SC q12h; age ≥ 75 years — no bolus, 0.75 mg/kg SC q12h; dose-adjust for CrCl < 30 mL/min.
    • OR Unfractionated Heparin 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4000 units) then 12 units/kg/h infusion (max 1000 units/h), titrated to aPTT 1.5–2.0 × control for 48 hours.

Execution of Fibrinolysis

  • Administer Tenecteplase (TNKase) as a single IV bolus over 5 seconds using weight-based dosing (see Tenecteplase Instructions).
  • Consider half-dose TNK for patients ≥ 75 years per local protocol (STREAM / STREAM-2 evidence of reduced intracranial hemorrhage).
  • Administer adjunctive ASA, P2Y12 inhibitor (Clopidogrel), and anticoagulant (Enoxaparin preferred, OR UFH).
  • Flush dextrose-containing lines with normal saline before and after TNK; avoid IM injections and non-compressible vascular punctures.

Post-Fibrinolysis (Pharmacoinvasive Strategy)

  • Reassess 12-lead ECG at 60 and 90 minutes. Criteria for successful reperfusion: resolution of chest pain, ≥ 50% reduction in ST elevation in the worst lead, and reperfusion arrhythmias (e.g., accelerated idioventricular rhythm).
  • Failed fibrinolysis (< 50% ST resolution at 60–90 min, ongoing ischemia, hemodynamic/electrical instability): activate emergent rescue PCI.
  • Successful fibrinolysis: transfer to a PCI-capable centre and plan coronary angiography within 2–24 hours (pharmacoinvasive approach).
  • Monitor for reperfusion arrhythmias, hypotension, bleeding (especially intracranial — any new neurologic deficit: stop anticoagulants, STAT CT head).

Complications to Anticipate

  • Ventricular arrhythmias (VF, VT, AIVR) — treat per ACLS.
  • Bradyarrhythmias / AV block (especially inferior MI) — atropine, transcutaneous pacing, chronotropic infusions (Dopamine or Epinephrine).
  • Cardiogenic shock — vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line), inotropes, early mechanical support (IABP, Impella, VA-ECMO) and emergent PCI.
  • Mechanical complications (papillary muscle rupture, VSD, free wall rupture) — bedside echo, emergent surgical consult.
  • Right ventricular infarction — avoid nitrates, give IV crystalloid bolus, consider inotropes.
Tenecteplase (TNK) Instructions

Indication

Fibrinolysis of coronary artery thrombosis associated with acute STEMI when primary PCI cannot be delivered within 120 minutes of first medical contact and symptom onset is ≤ 12 hours. Confirm the Fibrinolytic Checklist and obtain informed (or deferred) consent where feasible.

Reconstitution

  • Use the supplied 10 mL prefilled syringe of sterile water for injection; inject slowly into the TNK vial directing the stream at the powder.
  • Swirl gently to dissolve; do not shake (foaming inactivates the drug).
  • Final concentration: 5 mg/mL (50 mg in 10 mL). Solution should be clear and colourless to pale yellow.
  • Use immediately after reconstitution; if delay is unavoidable, may be stored refrigerated (2–8°C) for up to 8 hours.

Administration

  • Administer as a single IV bolus over 5 seconds via a dedicated peripheral line.
  • Flush dextrose-containing lines with 0.9% NS before AND after administration — TNK is incompatible with dextrose solutions and will precipitate.
  • Discard any unused reconstituted product.

Weight-Based Dosing Chart (Standard Dose)

  • Less than 60 kg: 30 mg (6 mL)
  • 60 to 69 kg: 35 mg (7 mL)
  • 70 to 79 kg: 40 mg (8 mL)
  • 80 to 89 kg: 45 mg (9 mL)
  • 90 kg or more: 50 mg (10 mL)

Half-Dose Tenecteplase for Patients ≥ 75 Years

Based on STREAM and STREAM-2 trial evidence and reflected in current Canadian regional STEMI protocols, patients ≥ 75 years have an increased risk of intracranial hemorrhage with full-dose fibrinolytics. Half-dose TNK significantly reduces ICH without loss of efficacy in the elderly.

  • Less than 60 kg: 15 mg (3 mL)
  • 60 to 69 kg: 17.5 mg (3.5 mL)
  • 70 to 79 kg: 20 mg (4 mL)
  • 80 to 89 kg: 22.5 mg (4.5 mL)
  • 90 kg or more: 25 mg (5 mL)

Apply only where endorsed by your regional STEMI / medical director protocol.

Adjunctive Therapy (give with TNK)

  • Aspirin 160–325 mg chewed, then 81 mg PO daily.
  • Clopidogrel: 300 mg PO loading if < 75 years; 75 mg PO if ≥ 75 years; then 75 mg PO daily.
  • Anticoagulation × up to 8 days or until revascularization:
    • Enoxaparin preferred: age < 75 — 30 mg IV bolus then 1 mg/kg SC q12h; age ≥ 75 — no IV bolus, 0.75 mg/kg SC q12h; reduce frequency to once daily if CrCl < 30 mL/min.
    • OR Unfractionated Heparin: 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4000 units) then 12 units/kg/h infusion (max 1000 units/h), titrate to aPTT 1.5–2.0 × control.

Monitoring

  • Vital signs and neuro checks q5min × 15 min after bolus; q15min × 1 h; q30min × 2 h; then q1h.
  • Repeat 12-lead ECG at 60 and 90 minutes — assess for ≥ 50% resolution of ST elevation in the worst lead.
  • Monitor continuously on cardiac monitor with defibrillator at the bedside (reperfusion arrhythmias are common — AIVR is benign; treat sustained VT/VF per ACLS).
  • Watch for bleeding: oral mucosa, IV sites, urine, stool, new neuro symptoms (stop anticoagulant/antiplatelet, activate CT head STAT, reverse with blood products, tranexamic acid, or cryoprecipitate per hematology).
  • Allergic/anaphylactoid reactions: hypotension, angioedema, bronchospasm — treat with IV fluids, Epinephrine, Diphenhydramine, and corticosteroids.
  • Avoid IM injections, arterial punctures, and non-compressible venous access for 24 hours.

Failed fibrinolysis (< 50% ST resolution at 60–90 min, ongoing ischemia, hemodynamic or electrical instability) → activate rescue PCI without delay.

Drug Medications for Acute MI Management

Clinical Indication

Quick-reference dosing for the medications used in adult acute coronary syndrome / STEMI care. Aligned with Canadian Cardiovascular Society / Heart & Stroke Foundation and AHA/ACC guidelines. Tap any linked medication name to open its full monograph in the Medication page.

Antiplatelet Therapy

  • Aspirin (ASA): 160 to 325 mg non-enteric-coated, chewed and swallowed; then 81 mg PO daily indefinitely. Inhibits platelet COX-1 / thromboxane A2.
  • P2Y12 Inhibitors (one agent, choose per reperfusion strategy):
    • Clopidogrel: 600 mg PO loading for primary PCI; 300 mg PO for fibrinolysis if < 75 years (75 mg if ≥ 75 years); maintenance 75 mg PO daily.
    • Ticagrelor: 180 mg PO loading then 90 mg PO BID (do not combine with ASA > 100 mg/day).
    • Prasugrel: 60 mg PO loading then 10 mg PO daily (PCI only; contraindicated with prior stroke/TIA, avoid if age ≥ 75 or weight < 60 kg).

Anticoagulation

  • Unfractionated Heparin: PCI — 70–100 units/kg IV bolus (50–70 units/kg with GPIIb/IIIa inhibitor), titrate to ACT. Fibrinolysis / NSTEMI — 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4000 units) then 12 units/kg/h infusion (max 1000 units/h), titrate aPTT 1.5–2.0 × control.
  • Enoxaparin: Age < 75 — 30 mg IV bolus then 1 mg/kg SC q12h. Age ≥ 75 — no IV bolus, 0.75 mg/kg SC q12h. Reduce to once-daily dosing if CrCl < 30 mL/min.

Anti-Ischemic / Symptom Control

  • Nitroglycerin: 0.4 mg SL spray or tablet q5min PRN (max 3 doses). IV infusion 10 mcg/min initial; titrate by 10 mcg/min q5min (max 200 mcg/min) maintaining SBP > 90 mmHg. Coronary and systemic vasodilation; reduces preload. Avoid with SBP < 90 mmHg, suspected RV infarction, HR < 50 or > 100, or recent PDE-5 inhibitor use.
  • Morphine: 1 to 5 mg IV q5–15min PRN for refractory ischemic pain. Analgesia, anxiolysis, venodilation. Use judiciously — may reduce P2Y12 absorption.
  • Fentanyl: 25 to 50 mcg IV q5–10min PRN as an alternative opioid analgesic; better hemodynamic stability than morphine.
  • Metoprolol: Oral preferred within the first 24 hours if stable (e.g., 25–50 mg PO q6–12h). IV 2.5–5 mg slow push q5min (up to 15 mg) reserved for persistent hypertension or ongoing ischemia; do not use in hypotension, bradycardia, heart failure signs, high-degree AV block, or active bronchospasm.

Fibrinolytic

  • Tenecteplase (TNKase): single IV bolus over 5 seconds, weight-based (30 mg < 60 kg → 50 mg ≥ 90 kg). Half-dose protocol for age ≥ 75 years per local policy (STREAM-2 evidence). Flush dextrose-containing lines with 0.9% NS before and after administration. See Tenecteplase Instructions for full dosing.

Supportive / Adjunct Therapy

  • Dimenhydrinate: 25 to 50 mg IV (max rate 25 mg/min) or PO q4h PRN for nausea/vomiting. Ondansetron 4 mg IV is an alternative.
  • Lorazepam: 1 mg PO/SL q4h PRN for anxiety or restlessness.
  • Potassium Chloride: supplement to maintain K+ ≥ 4.0 mmol/L (per medical director orders if K+ < 3.5 mmol/L).
  • Magnesium Sulfate: 1 to 2 g IV over 15 min for documented hypomagnesemia, torsades de pointes, or refractory VT/VF.
  • Furosemide: 20 to 80 mg IV for acute pulmonary edema / volume overload complicating MI.

Arrhythmia / Peri-Arrest

  • Amiodarone: cardiac arrest (VF/pVT) — 300 mg IV/IO bolus, then 150 mg if refractory. Stable wide-complex VT — 150 mg IV over 10 min, then 1 mg/min × 6 h then 0.5 mg/min.
  • Lidocaine: 1 to 1.5 mg/kg IV/IO first dose, 0.5 to 0.75 mg/kg IV/IO repeat dose q5–10min (max 3 mg/kg); alternative to amiodarone in VF/pVT.
  • Epinephrine: cardiac arrest — 1 mg IV/IO q3–5min. Push-dose pressor for severe hypotension — 5 to 20 mcg IV q2–5min. Infusion for cardiogenic shock — 2 to 10 mcg/min, titrate to MAP ≥ 65 mmHg.
  • Norepinephrine: first-line vasopressor for cardiogenic shock — 0.05–0.5 mcg/kg/min (start 2 to 4 mcg/min), titrate q10–15min to MAP ≥ 65 mmHg.
  • Dopamine: 5 to 20 mcg/kg/min IV infusion for symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to atropine, or as an alternative in bradycardic hypotension.
  • Labetalol: 10 to 20 mg IV (or infusion 0.5–2 mg/min) for severe hypertension delaying fibrinolysis (target SBP < 180 / DBP < 110 mmHg); avoid in heart failure, bradycardia, or bronchospasm.

Allergic Reaction to Fibrinolytic

  • Epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg IM (1:1000) for anaphylaxis; IV infusion for refractory shock.
  • Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV, plus H2 blocker (e.g., Famotidine 20 mg IV), plus corticosteroid (e.g., Dexamethasone 10 mg IV).
Airway Management & LEMON Assessment

Clinical Indication

Structured pre-intubation assessment to predict difficulty with bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation, supraglottic device (SGA) placement, or endotracheal intubation. Aligned with the Canadian Airway Focus Group (CAFG) 2021 guidelines and the Difficult Airway Society (DAS) principles adopted in Canadian emergency departments.

Pre-Intubation Assessment — LEMON

  • L — Look externally: Facial trauma, large incisors, beard/moustache, enlarged tongue (macroglossia), receding mandible (retrognathia), short muscular neck, high arched palate, morbid obesity, or abnormal facial shape (e.g., Pierre Robin, Treacher Collins).
  • E — Evaluate (3-3-2 Rule):
    • Inter-incisor distance ≥ 3 finger-breadths (≥ 4 cm).
    • Hyomental distance ≥ 3 finger-breadths (tip of mentum to hyoid).
    • Thyrohyoid distance ≥ 2 finger-breadths (hyoid to thyroid notch).
  • M — Mallampati Score: Class I (full soft palate, uvula, pillars) through IV (hard palate only). Class III or IV predicts a difficult laryngoscopic view.
  • O — Obstruction / Obesity: Stridor, muffled voice, drooling, angioedema, epiglottitis, Ludwig's angina, peritonsillar abscess, airway burns/inhalation injury, expanding neck hematoma, foreign body, or BMI > 35 kg/m².
  • N — Neck Mobility: Ability to achieve the sniffing position (atlanto-occipital extension ≥ 35°). Limited by c-collar, trauma, ankylosing spondylitis, rheumatoid arthritis, Down syndrome (atlanto-axial instability), or prior cervical fusion.

Difficult BVM Predictors (MOANS)

  • Mask seal difficulty (beard, facial trauma)
  • Obese / obstruction
  • Aged > 55 years
  • No teeth
  • Stiff lungs / stridor (asthma, ARDS, pulmonary edema)

Difficult SGA Predictors (RODS)

  • Restricted mouth opening
  • Obstruction (upper airway)
  • Distorted airway anatomy
  • Stiff lungs or stiff c-spine

Difficult Cricothyrotomy Predictors (SMART)

  • Surgery (prior neck surgery / scar)
  • Mass (hematoma, abscess, tumour)
  • Access problems (obesity, short neck)
  • Radiation (distorted tissue planes)
  • Tumour

Management Algorithm (CAFG-Aligned)

  1. Plan A — Optimal first-attempt intubation: perform LEMON/MOANS/RODS/SMART; call for help early if difficulty predicted; optimize patient position (ear-to-sternal-notch, ramped if obese), pre-oxygenate with high-flow O₂ for ≥ 3 min, apply apneic oxygenation (nasal cannula 15 L/min); use video laryngoscopy (VL) as first-line per CAFG 2021; use a bougie routinely.
  2. Plan B — Maintain oxygenation via SGA: insert a 2nd-generation SGA (e.g., i-gel, LMA ProSeal, LMA Supreme). Limit to 3 attempts.
  3. Plan C — Two-handed BVM with adjuncts: OPA + NPA, 2-person technique, PEEP valve. Wake the patient if feasible.
  4. Plan D — "Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate" (CICO) — Front-of-Neck Access (FONA): Scalpel-bougie-tube cricothyrotomy (preferred technique in DAS/CAFG) using a #10 blade through cricothyroid membrane, then bougie, then 6.0 cuffed ETT. Ensure surgical airway kit (e.g., Melker or scalpel-finger-bougie set) is open at the bedside before induction in any predicted-difficult airway.
  5. Declare CICO verbally to the team and commit to FONA without delay once SpO₂ cannot be maintained by any other means.

Key Canadian Guideline Points

  • Video laryngoscopy is recommended as first-line for all emergency intubations (CAFG 2021).
  • Awake tracheal intubation (ATI) under topical anaesthesia should be considered in the cooperative patient with predicted difficult airway.
  • Limit intubation attempts to 3 by the most experienced operator before moving to rescue techniques.
  • Continuous waveform capnography is mandatory for confirmation and ongoing monitoring (CAEP Position Statement).
Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI) Checklist

Clinical Indication

Near-simultaneous administration of a potent induction agent and a rapid-acting neuromuscular blocker to secure a definitive airway while minimizing the risk of aspiration and peri-intubation hypoxemia/hypotension. Aligned with Canadian Airway Focus Group (CAFG) 2021 and CAEP airway best-practice recommendations.

The 7 P's of RSI

  1. Preparation (Zero minus 10 minutes) — "SOAP-ME":
    • Suction (Yankauer connected, on, tested) x 2 if available.
    • Oxygen (non-rebreather at flush rate + nasal cannula at 15 L/min for apneic oxygenation).
    • Airway equipment: BVM with PEEP valve, OPA/NPA, video laryngoscope (primary, per CAFG 2021) and direct laryngoscope (backup), ETTs (sized and one smaller), stylet, bougie, 10 mL cuff syringe, ETT holder, 2nd-generation SGA (e.g., i-gel), and an opened scalpel-bougie cricothyrotomy kit.
    • Pharmacy: induction, paralytic, push-dose pressor, and post-intubation sedation/analgesia drawn up and labelled.
    • Monitors: continuous ECG, SpO₂, NIBP q1–3 min, continuous waveform capnography (ETCO₂) — mandatory.
    • Equipment check + team briefing (roles, shared mental model, Plans A–D, verbalized failed-airway trigger).
    • Ensure 2 functioning IV/IO sites; consider arterial line in the hemodynamically tenuous.
  2. Preoxygenation (Zero minus 5 minutes):
    • 100% O₂ via NRB at flush rate (≥ 15 L/min) or BVM with PEEP valve for ≥ 3 minutes (or 8 vital-capacity breaths). Target SpO₂ ≥ 95% (≥ 98% if possible).
    • Apply nasal cannula at 15 L/min for apneic oxygenation; leave in place throughout the apneic period (NO DESAT / THRIVE concept).
    • Consider NIV/CPAP/BiPAP in shunt physiology (pneumonia, ARDS, CPE) or DSI (delayed sequence intubation) with Ketamine 1 mg/kg IV if the patient is too agitated to cooperate with preoxygenation.
    • Head-up/ramped position (20–30°) unless c-spine precautions.
  3. Pretreatment / Optimization (Zero minus 3 minutes):
    • Resuscitate before you intubate: correct hypotension (target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg) with crystalloid bolus and/or push-dose Epinephrine 10–20 mcg IV q2–5 min; have a Phenylephrine 100–200 mcg IV push-dose alternative for tachycardia-sensitive patients.
    • Blunt sympathetic response with Fentanyl 1–3 mcg/kg IV (over 30–60 s) in raised ICP, aortic dissection, or severe coronary disease (omit if hypotensive/shocked).
    • Consider Lidocaine 1.5 mg/kg IV 3 min pre-induction in severe bronchospasm (evidence is limited; optional).
  4. Paralysis with Induction (Time = 0):
    • Induction agents:
      • Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV push over 30–60 s (preferred in shock, status asthmaticus, reactive airway disease; reduce to 0.5–1 mg/kg in profound shock/catecholamine-depleted states).
      • Etomidate 0.2–0.3 mg/kg IV (hemodynamically neutral; single-dose adrenal suppression is generally clinically inconsequential).
      • Propofol 1.5–2.5 mg/kg IV (reserve for the stable/hypertensive patient; causes vasodilation and myocardial depression).
      • Midazolam 0.2–0.3 mg/kg IV is a less-preferred alternative (slower onset, less reliable anaesthesia).
    • Neuromuscular blocker (immediately after induction):
      • Succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg IV (2 mg/kg IV if shock; 3–4 mg/kg IM if no IV). Onset 45–60 s, duration 6–10 min. Contraindicated: known/suspected hyperkalemia, burn or crush > 48–72 h old, denervation (stroke, SCI) > 72 h, neuromuscular disease (ALS, muscular dystrophy), personal/family history of malignant hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis.
      • Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg IV (1.5 mg/kg in shock). Onset ~45–60 s at this dose, duration 45–70 min. Reversible with Sugammadex 16 mg/kg IV if immediate reversal is required.
  5. Positioning (Zero plus 30 seconds):
    • Ear-to-sternal-notch alignment (ramped for obese patients); sniffing position if no c-spine concern.
    • Maintain manual in-line stabilization (MILS) with c-collar opened anteriorly for trauma patients; cricoid pressure is optional and should be released if it worsens the view.
  6. Placement with Proof (Zero plus 45–60 seconds):
    • Perform video laryngoscopy (first-line per CAFG 2021); use a bougie routinely (Bougie Use Meets Predicted Easy Routine).
    • Optimize view using the Kovacs Kata sequence if needed:
      • Neck: external laryngeal manipulation / BURP (Backward, Upward, Rightward Pressure).
      • Head: lift the occiput off the bed.
      • Hands: use two hands on the laryngoscope to increase lift.
      • Scoop: reposition blade to lift a floppy epiglottis.
      • Pull-back: pull back slightly with a hyper-angulated VL blade to widen the view.
    • Pass the ETT, inflate the cuff (pilot balloon palpation or cuff manometer 20–30 cmH₂O), and confirm placement with:
      • Sustained waveform ETCO₂ over ≥ 6 breaths (gold standard).
      • Auscultation bilaterally + over the epigastrium.
      • Tube misting, chest rise, SpO₂ trend.
      • Post-intubation CXR for depth confirmation (tip 2–4 cm above carina).
    • Maximum 3 attempts by the most experienced operator before escalating to Plan B/C/D.
  7. Post-Intubation Management:
    • Secure the ETT; document depth at the incisors (typically 21 cm F / 23 cm M).
    • Lung-protective ventilation: VT 6–8 mL/kg IBW, plateau pressure < 30 cmH₂O, PEEP 5 (titrate), FiO₂ to SpO₂ 92–96%, titrate RR to ETCO₂ 35–40 (avoid hyperventilation except as a brief temporizing measure in impending herniation).
    • Reassess vital signs every 1–3 min for the first 15 min; anticipate and treat post-intubation hypotension (positive-pressure ventilation drops preload).
    • Analgosedation (analgesia-first strategy):
      • Analgesia: Fentanyl 25–100 mcg IV bolus, then infusion 25–200 mcg/h; or Hydromorphone 0.2–0.5 mg IV q15–30 min PRN.
      • Sedation: Propofol 5–50 mcg/kg/min IV (caution with hypotension), Ketamine infusion 0.5–2 mg/kg/h IV (hemodynamic-sparing), or Midazolam 1–10 mg/h IV (avoid prolonged use — delirium / delayed emergence).
      • Target RASS −2 to 0; use validated scales (CPOT/BPS for pain, CAM-ICU for delirium).
    • Orogastric tube and DVT/stress ulcer prophylaxis per local ICU bundle.

Failed Airway Triggers

  • 3 failed intubation attempts by the most experienced operator, OR
  • Inability to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 90% with BVM + adjuncts, OR
  • "Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate" (CICO) — declare verbally and proceed directly to front-of-neck access (scalpel-bougie cricothyrotomy).
Tracheal Intubation of Critically Ill Adults

Clinical Indication

Intubation of patients with severe physiologic derangements (shock, severe hypoxemia, profound acidosis, or combined "physiologically difficult" airway) who are at high risk for peri-intubation cardiovascular collapse or cardiac arrest. Based on CAFG 2021, PREOXI, and INTUBE cohort recommendations applicable to Canadian EDs/ICUs.

Identify the "Physiologically Difficult" Airway (HOP-K)

  • H — Hypotension / Shock Index > 0.9: SBP < 100 mmHg or SI (HR/SBP) > 0.9 predicts peri-intubation collapse.
  • O — Oxygenation failure: SpO₂ < 93% on high-FiO₂ or P/F < 200 (shunt physiology).
  • P — pH / acidosis: pH < 7.25 or severe compensatory tachypnea (cannot tolerate apnea).
  • K — RV strain / cardiac dysfunction: massive PE, severe pulmonary hypertension, severe aortic stenosis, or acute RV failure.

1. Hemodynamic Optimization (Resuscitate Before You Intubate)

  • Volume status: assess with POCUS (IVC collapsibility, LV function, lung B-lines). Administer a 500–1000 mL crystalloid bolus (balanced solution such as Ringer's lactate preferred over 0.9% NaCl) unless frank pulmonary edema or cardiogenic shock.
  • Correct hypotension before induction: target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg (higher in chronic hypertension, TBI, or spinal cord injury).
    • Push-dose Epinephrine 10–20 mcg IV q2–5 min (mix 1 mg in 100 mL = 10 mcg/mL) — first-line for most shock states.
    • Push-dose Phenylephrine 100–200 mcg IV q2–5 min (mix 10 mg in 100 mL = 100 mcg/mL) — consider in tachycardia or diastolic dysfunction.
    • Initiate continuous norepinephrine infusion (0.05–0.5 mcg/kg/min; typical start 2–8 mcg/min) prior to induction; alternatives include Epinephrine infusion 2–10 mcg/min or Vasopressin 0.03 units/min as an adjunct.
  • Blood products in hemorrhagic shock (activate massive transfusion per local Canadian Blood Services protocol; target 1:1:1 RBC:FFP:platelets) and tranexamic acid (TXA) 1 g IV within 3 h of traumatic hemorrhage.
  • Obtain an arterial line before or shortly after intubation in any shocked patient.

2. Preoxygenation & Apneic Oxygenation

  • Use flush-rate O₂ (≥ 40–60 L/min) via NRB + nasal cannula at 15 L/min for ≥ 3 minutes (or 8 vital-capacity breaths).
  • For shunt physiology (ARDS, pneumonia, CPE) use NIV/CPAP 5–10 cmH₂O or BVM + PEEP valve (5–10 cmH₂O) during preoxygenation (PREOXI trial supports NIV over NRB in hypoxemic patients).
  • Maintain nasal cannula at 15 L/min throughout the apneic period (passive apneic oxygenation).
  • Position head-up / ramped 20–30° unless c-spine precautions.
  • Consider delayed sequence intubation (DSI) with Ketamine 1 mg/kg IV if agitation precludes preoxygenation.

3. Induction & Paralysis Dose Adjustments in Shock

  • Preferred induction: Ketamine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV (sympathomimetic). Reduce to 0.5–1 mg/kg IV in profound shock / catecholamine-depleted states to avoid direct myocardial depression.
  • Alternative: Etomidate 0.15–0.3 mg/kg IV (reduce 50% in shock). Transient adrenal suppression after a single dose is generally clinically insignificant; ensure adequate steroid coverage (e.g., Dexamethasone 4–8 mg IV) if septic shock and the patient remains catecholamine-dependent.
  • Avoid or dose-reduce: Propofol and Midazolam cause vasodilation / myocardial depression; if used, halve the dose.
  • Paralysis: use a full or increased dose due to slower circulation time in shock:
    • Rocuronium 1.5–1.6 mg/kg IV (preferred in shock; no hyperkalemia risk; longer duration provides a margin of safety). Reverse with Sugammadex 16 mg/kg IV if needed.
    • Succinylcholine 2 mg/kg IV (increased dose in low cardiac output). Avoid if hyperkalemia is suspected (renal failure, rhabdomyolysis, crush/burn injury, chronic denervation).

4. Execution — Maximize First-Pass Success

  • Use video laryngoscopy first-line (CAFG 2021).
  • Use a bougie on the first attempt.
  • Most experienced operator performs the first attempt; limit to 3 attempts before escalating.
  • Declare CICO and proceed to scalpel-bougie-tube cricothyrotomy without delay if oxygenation cannot be maintained.

5. Post-Intubation

  • Confirm with sustained waveform ETCO₂, auscultation, chest rise, and post-procedure CXR.
  • Anticipate hypotension: positive-pressure ventilation reduces preload. Have pressors running or drawn up; reassess BP within 1–2 min and q3–5 min thereafter.
  • Lung-protective ventilation: VT 6–8 mL/kg IBW, Pplat < 30 cmH₂O, PEEP 5 cmH₂O (titrate in ARDS), FiO₂ titrated to SpO₂ 92–96% (88–92% if ARDS/COPD), RR titrated to match pre-intubation minute ventilation in severe acidosis (avoid breath-stacking in asthma/COPD — permissive hypercapnia).
  • Analgosedation:
    • Fentanyl bolus 25–100 mcg IV, then infusion 25–200 mcg/h; or Hydromorphone 0.2–0.5 mg IV PRN.
    • Ketamine infusion 0.5–2 mg/kg/h IV (hemodynamic-sparing, first-line in shock/bronchospasm).
    • Propofol 5–50 mcg/kg/min (caution with hypotension).
    • Midazolam only if other agents unavailable; avoid prolonged use.
  • Orogastric tube, head-of-bed 30–45°, DVT and stress ulcer prophylaxis (e.g., Pantoprazole 40 mg IV daily) per local ICU bundle.

Key Canadian Guideline Pearls

  • Hypotension, not hypoxia, is the leading peri-intubation driver of cardiac arrest (INTUBE study, AJRCCM 2021).
  • Avoid hyperventilation except briefly in impending cerebral herniation.
  • Target SpO₂ 92–96% post-intubation (ICU-ROX trial); avoid hyperoxia.
  • In septic shock, initiate antibiotics (per local protocol, e.g., Piperacillin/Tazobactam ± Vasopressin) — intubation does not replace source control.
Combative Patient Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Management of a patient exhibiting severe agitation, excited delirium (hyperactive delirium with severe agitation — HDSA), or violent behaviour that poses an immediate threat to the safety of the patient, staff, or bystanders. Aligned with CAEP Position Statement on Acute Agitation in the ED, CPA (Canadian Psychiatric Association) guidelines, and Project BETA (Best practices in Evaluation and Treatment of Agitation) adopted in Canadian EDs.

Differential Diagnosis — Treat Reversible Causes First

  • Medical: hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hypo/hypernatremia, uremia, hepatic encephalopathy, thyroid storm, sepsis, meningitis/encephalitis, non-convulsive status epilepticus.
  • Neurologic: traumatic brain injury, intracranial hemorrhage, stroke, post-ictal state.
  • Toxicologic: sympathomimetic (cocaine, methamphetamine, MDMA), hallucinogen (PCP, ketamine, NPS), anticholinergic, serotonin syndrome, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, salicylate toxicity, alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal.
  • Psychiatric: psychosis, mania, severe personality disorder, autism spectrum dysregulation.

1. Safety & Verbal De-escalation (Project BETA)

  • Ensure scene safety; maintain a clear exit path; remove potential weapons from the room (pens, stethoscopes, sharps).
  • Call security early; assemble a restraint team of ≥ 5 trained personnel (one per limb + airway/leader) before approach.
  • Verbal de-escalation (10 domains — Project BETA): respect personal space, do not be provocative, establish verbal contact, be concise, identify wants/feelings, listen closely, agree or agree to disagree, set clear limits, offer choices and optimism, debrief.
  • Offer oral medication as a first-line chemical option if the patient can cooperate.

2. Physical Restraint (Only If Immediate Danger Exists)

  • Minimum 5 trained personnel; one dedicated team leader controlling the head/airway.
  • Apply 4-point soft limb restraints in the supine position; head of bed 30°. Never use the prone "hobble" position — risk of positional asphyxia / sudden cardiac death.
  • Document indication, least-restrictive alternatives attempted, time applied, and plan for reassessment q15 min.
  • Remove restraints as soon as clinically safe; chemical sedation is preferred over prolonged physical restraint.

3. Chemical Restraint (Canadian ED Choices)

Oral (cooperative patient):

  • Lorazepam 1–2 mg PO/SL. Onset 30–60 min. Useful in alcohol/sedative withdrawal.
  • Haloperidol 5–10 mg PO (2.5–5 mg if elderly or frail).
  • Risperidone 1–2 mg PO (if available).
  • Olanzapine ODT 5–10 mg PO/SL (if available).

Moderate agitation (IM / IV):

  • Haloperidol 5–10 mg IM or IV (2.5 mg in elderly). Onset IM 15–30 min, IV 5–10 min. Monitor QTc (hold if QTc > 500 ms, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia). May combine with Lorazepam 2 mg IM/IV for synergistic effect ("5 and 2").
  • Midazolam 5–10 mg IM or 1–2.5 mg IV. Fastest onset of the benzodiazepines (IM 5–10 min). Monitor for respiratory depression.
  • Lorazepam 2–4 mg IM/IV (preferred in hepatic dysfunction; predictable IM absorption).
  • Diazepam 5–10 mg IV (first-line in alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal).
  • Consider adjuncts: Benztropine 1–2 mg IM/IV or Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV for acute dystonia from antipsychotics.

Severe / Violent Agitation or Excited Delirium (rapid control):

  • Ketamine 4–5 mg/kg IM (onset 2–5 min) or 1–2 mg/kg IV (onset < 1 min). Preferred when IV access is not feasible and rapid control is required. Anticipate laryngospasm, hypersalivation, emergence phenomenon, transient hypertension/tachycardia, and a ∼30% rate of peri-intubation respiratory depression requiring BVM/intubation. Avoid as sole agent if known schizophrenia.
  • Midazolam 10 mg IM in combination with Haloperidol 5–10 mg IM is a widely used Canadian alternative.
  • Droperidol (if stocked) 5–10 mg IM/IV — availability in Canada is limited; Haloperidol is the standard substitute.

Special populations:

  • Elderly / delirium: start with 25–50% of the adult dose (e.g., Haloperidol 0.5–2 mg IM); avoid benzodiazepines except in withdrawal (paradoxical agitation, falls).
  • Pregnancy: Haloperidol is generally safe; benzodiazepines in late pregnancy may cause neonatal withdrawal. Avoid ketamine if possible.
  • Alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal: benzodiazepines are first-line (Diazepam 10–20 mg IV q10 min titrated to CIWA, or Lorazepam 2–4 mg IV q10–15 min). Add Thiamine 100–500 mg IV before dextrose.
  • Prolonged QT / known heart disease: avoid haloperidol / droperidol / IV antipsychotics; prefer benzodiazepines or ketamine.
  • Cocaine / sympathomimetic toxicity: benzodiazepines first-line; avoid beta-blockers (unopposed alpha); Ketamine is an acceptable alternative.

4. Post-Sedation Monitoring & Work-up

  • Assess ABCs immediately; establish continuous SpO₂, ECG, BP q5 min, continuous waveform ETCO₂ for any parenteral sedation.
  • Core temperature (rectal preferred) — hyperthermia ≥ 39°C in excited delirium requires aggressive active cooling and ICU admission.
  • Capillary blood glucose on arrival; treat hypoglycemia with Dextrose 25 g IV (50 mL of D50W); give Thiamine 100 mg IV first in suspected chronic alcohol use/malnutrition.
  • Baseline investigations: CBC, electrolytes, creatinine/urea, glucose, CK, lactate, ABG/VBG, troponin, ECG (QTc), urine drug screen, β-hCG, blood alcohol, acetaminophen/salicylate, ammonia if indicated.
  • Imaging (CT head) for unexplained altered mental status, focal neurologic deficit, head trauma, anticoagulation, or failure to normalize after sedation.
  • Reassess need for continued restraint q15 min; document response; switch to least-restrictive alternative; involve psychiatry and social work early.
  • Aspiration precautions (HOB 30°, suction at bedside) and DVT prophylaxis if prolonged immobility.
Burn Estimation Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Assessment of Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) affected by partial-thickness (2nd degree) and full-thickness (3rd and 4th degree) burns to guide fluid resuscitation, analgesia, and transfer to a burn centre. Aligned with ATLS 10th/11th Edition (Canadian course) and the American Burn Association / Canadian Burn Association criteria used by regional Canadian burn centres (e.g., Ross Tilley Sunnybrook, Vancouver GH, Foothills, HSC Winnipeg, CHU de Québec).

Note: Do not include superficial (1st-degree) burns in TBSA calculations for fluid resuscitation.

1. Rule of Nines (Adults ≥ 16 yr)

  • Head and Neck: 9%
  • Anterior Trunk: 18%
  • Posterior Trunk: 18%
  • Each Upper Extremity: 9% (x2 = 18%)
  • Each Lower Extremity: 18% (x2 = 36%)
  • Perineum / Genitalia: 1%

Pediatric Modifications (Lund & Browder Chart preferred)

  • Infant / child: head is proportionally larger (18% in infants) and legs smaller (14% each in infants).
  • Use a Lund & Browder chart for accuracy in children (reduces overestimation that commonly leads to over-resuscitation).

2. Rule of Palms (Scattered / Irregular Burns)

The patient's palmar surface including fingers ≈ 1% TBSA (palm alone ≈ 0.5%).

3. Depth Assessment

  • Superficial (1st Degree): epidermis only. Erythema, painful, blanches, no blisters. Heals in 3–7 days. Exclude from TBSA for fluid calculation.
  • Superficial Partial Thickness (Superficial 2nd Degree): epidermis + superficial dermis. Red/pink, moist, blistered, very painful, blanches. Heals in 7–21 days with minimal scarring.
  • Deep Partial Thickness (Deep 2nd Degree): epidermis + deep dermis. White/red mottled, less painful, poor capillary refill, often requires grafting.
  • Full Thickness (3rd Degree): through dermis. Leathery, dry, waxy white / charred, painless, non-blanching. Requires excision and grafting.
  • Subdermal (4th Degree): extends into fascia, muscle, bone. Usually requires complex reconstruction or amputation.

4. Canadian Burn Centre Referral Criteria (ABA / CBA)

  • Partial-thickness burns > 10% TBSA.
  • Full-thickness burns of any size.
  • Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints.
  • Electrical (including lightning) or chemical burns.
  • Inhalation injury.
  • Burns in patients with pre-existing illness that could complicate management, prolong recovery, or affect mortality.
  • Burn with concomitant trauma (e.g., fractures) in which the burn poses the greatest risk of morbidity/mortality.
  • Pediatric patients in facilities without pediatric-trained personnel.
  • Patients requiring special social, emotional, or rehabilitative intervention.

5. Associated Injury Assessment

  • Inhalation injury: enclosed-space fire, singed nasal/facial hair, soot in oro-/nasopharynx, hoarseness, stridor, carbonaceous sputum, hypoxemia, or GCS drop. Low threshold for early intubation before airway edema develops.
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning: measure carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) — standard pulse oximetry is falsely reassuring. Treat with 100% O₂ via NRB; consider hyperbaric O₂ for COHb > 25%, LOC, neurological deficit, pregnancy with COHb > 15%, ischemic ECG changes, or metabolic acidosis. Consult toxicology/hyperbaric service.
  • Cyanide toxicity (closed-space combustion of plastics): suspect with persistent lactic acidosis (> 10 mmol/L) despite resuscitation, or "cherry red" venous blood. Give Sodium Thiosulfate 12.5 g IV (1 g/kg in children) ± hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) 5 g IV over 15 min if available.
  • Circumferential burns: assess for compartment syndrome — escharotomy (chest, limbs, digits) if compromised ventilation or perfusion.
  • Electrical injury: obtain 12-lead ECG and continuous cardiac monitoring; check CK and urine myoglobin; target urine output 1–1.5 mL/kg/h.
  • Tetanus prophylaxis: administer Td/Tdap if last booster > 5 years or uncertain.

6. Initial Wound & Supportive Care

  • Stop the burning process (remove clothing/jewellery; brush off dry chemicals; irrigate wet chemicals with copious water or saline).
  • Cover burns with clean, dry sheets; avoid ice / prolonged cold irrigation (risk of hypothermia, deepening injury).
  • Maintain normothermia: warm blankets, warmed IV fluids, warm room (ambient ≥ 28°C).
  • Analgesia: titrated IV opioids (e.g., Morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg IV q10 min or Fentanyl 0.5–1 mcg/kg IV q10 min; Hydromorphone 0.2–0.5 mg IV is a reasonable alternative). Consider low-dose Ketamine 0.1–0.3 mg/kg IV for opioid-sparing analgesia in major burns.
  • Antiemetic: Ondansetron 4–8 mg IV or Dimenhydrinate 25–50 mg IV.
  • Foley catheter for TBSA > 20% (urine output is the primary resuscitation endpoint — see next algorithm).
Major Burn Initial 24-Hour Fluid Resuscitation

Clinical Indication

Adults with ≥ 20% TBSA partial- or full-thickness burns (≥ 10% in children, elderly, or patients with significant comorbidity) require structured IV fluid resuscitation to prevent burn shock while avoiding over-resuscitation ("fluid creep"). Aligned with ATLS 11th Edition, American Burn Association consensus, and the Canadian Burn Association transfer-ready standard.

1. Fluid Selection

  • Preferred crystalloid: Ringer's Lactate (Lactated Ringer's / Hartmann's solution) — balanced electrolyte composition, minimizes hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis seen with 0.9% NaCl.
  • Avoid routine colloid (albumin, hydroxyethyl starch) in the first 12–24 h; colloid rescue may be considered after 12–24 h for refractory shock or excessive crystalloid requirement (> 6 mL/kg/%TBSA — "Ivy index").
  • Do not use hypotonic fluids or dextrose-containing solutions for initial resuscitation in adults.

2. Standard Calculation — Modified Parkland Formula (ATLS 11th Ed.)

  • Thermal / Chemical Burns: 2 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA over 24 h (recently revised down from 4 mL/kg/%TBSA to reduce over-resuscitation).
  • Electrical / High-Voltage Burns: 4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA over 24 h (higher baseline due to occult deep tissue injury and myoglobinuria).
  • Inhalation injury: typically requires 30–50% more fluid than calculated.

3. Administration Schedule

  • Give 50% of calculated total volume over the first 8 hours from the time of injury (not from arrival — subtract elapsed time).
  • Give the remaining 50% over the next 16 hours.
  • Example: 80 kg adult with 40% TBSA thermal burn → 2 × 80 × 40 = 6,400 mL Ringer's Lactate over 24 h; give 3,200 mL in first 8 h (400 mL/h), then 3,200 mL over next 16 h (200 mL/h).

4. Rule of 10s (ABA/Canadian Military — Prolonged Field Care / Initial Rate)

  • %TBSA rounded to the nearest 10 × 10 = initial RL rate in mL/h for adults 40–80 kg.
  • For every 10 kg > 80 kg, add 100 mL/h.
  • Example: 50% TBSA in a 90 kg adult → (50 × 10) + 100 = 600 mL/h initial RL rate. Re-calculate formal total at first reassessment.

5. Pediatric Resuscitation (< 16 yr)

  • 3 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA Ringer's Lactate over 24 h (half in first 8 h).
  • Add maintenance fluids (children have limited glycogen stores): 4-2-1 rule of D5LR (< 30 kg) — 4 mL/kg/h for first 10 kg, 2 mL/kg/h for next 10 kg, 1 mL/kg/h beyond.
  • Target urine output 1 mL/kg/h (1–2 mL/kg/h if < 30 kg).

6. Titration & Monitoring (the critical step)

  • Target urine output (thermal / chemical): 0.5 mL/kg/h (approximately 30–50 mL/h in a 70–80 kg adult).
  • Target urine output (electrical / rhabdomyolysis / myoglobinuria): 1–1.5 mL/kg/h (75–100 mL/h) until urine clears.
  • Reassess hourly: titrate rate up or down by 20–25% based on urine output; avoid arbitrary boluses unless the patient is clinically hypotensive.
  • Additional endpoints: MAP ≥ 65 mmHg, HR < 120, lactate downtrending, normal mentation, base deficit improving.
  • Consider arterial line and central venous access in TBSA > 30%, hemodynamic instability, or pressor requirement.
  • Warning signs of over-resuscitation ("fluid creep"): intra-abdominal hypertension (> 12 mmHg) / abdominal compartment syndrome, extremity compartment syndrome, pulmonary edema, orbital compartment syndrome. Urine output > 1 mL/kg/h in thermal burn resuscitation warrants reduction in rate.

7. Vasopressor / Adjunct Use

  • Norepinephrine infusion (0.05–0.5 mcg/kg/min) first-line for refractory hypotension despite 6 mL/kg/%TBSA crystalloid; alternative Vasopressin 0.03 units/min as adjunct.
  • Epinephrine push-dose 10–20 mcg IV for transient severe hypotension.
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) 66 mg/kg/h IV for the first 24 h has been studied for reducing crystalloid volume requirements (evidence limited; institutional protocol-dependent).

8. Associated Care

  • Foley catheter for hourly urine measurement; OG tube if > 20% TBSA or ileus/vomiting.
  • Glycemic control: target 6.1–10 mmol/L; consider Regular Insulin infusion in hyperglycemia.
  • Stress ulcer prophylaxis (e.g., Pantoprazole 40 mg IV daily).
  • Early enteral feeding (within 24 h) when hemodynamically stable to reduce bacterial translocation.
  • DVT prophylaxis once bleeding risk acceptable (mechanical + pharmacologic, e.g., Enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily — may need higher dosing in burn patients due to altered pharmacokinetics).
  • Analgesia per burn-pain protocol (Morphine, Fentanyl, Hydromorphone, low-dose Ketamine); consider multimodal approach (ketorolac avoided in acute resuscitation due to AKI risk).
  • Tetanus prophylaxis; do not administer prophylactic systemic antibiotics.
  • Temperature control: maintain core ≥ 36.5°C; warmed IV fluids, forced-air warming, ambient room temperature 28–32°C.
  • Notify the receiving burn centre early; follow the ABA Burn Referral Form / provincial transfer protocol.
Diagnose and Manage Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Clinical Indication

Adult patient with absolute or relative insulin deficiency presenting with hyperglycemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis. Based on Diabetes Canada 2018 Clinical Practice Guidelines — Chapter 15: Hyperglycemic Emergencies in Adults (with 2023 update addenda). Classic symptoms: polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations, acetone breath, altered mental status.

Common Precipitants — "The 6 I's"

  • Infection (pneumonia, UTI, sepsis)
  • Infarction (MI, stroke, mesenteric)
  • Insulin omission / non-adherence
  • Iatrogenic (glucocorticoids, thiazides, atypical antipsychotics, SGLT2 inhibitors — euglycemic DKA)
  • Initial presentation (new-onset type 1 diabetes)
  • Intoxication (cocaine, alcohol) / Intra-abdominal (pancreatitis)

1. Diagnostic Criteria (Diabetes Canada 2018)

  • Hyperglycemia: plasma glucose ≥ 14 mmol/L (usually; may be < 14 in euglycemic DKA — pregnancy, SGLT2 inhibitors, fasting).
  • Ketosis: β-hydroxybutyrate ≥ 3 mmol/L (preferred) or moderate-large urine ketones.
  • Anion-gap acidosis: pH < 7.30, serum HCO₃⁻ < 18 mmol/L, anion gap > 12 (AG = Na⁺ − [Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻]).

Severity:

  • Mild: pH 7.25–7.30, HCO₃⁻ 15–18, alert.
  • Moderate: pH 7.00–7.24, HCO₃⁻ 10–14, alert/drowsy.
  • Severe: pH < 7.00, HCO₃⁻ < 10, stuporous/coma.

DKA vs HHS: HHS (hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state) typically has glucose > 33 mmol/L, osmolality > 320 mOsm/kg, pH > 7.30, minimal ketones, and greater fluid deficit (8–10 L).

2. Initial Investigations

  • VBG (adequate vs ABG for pH & HCO₃⁻), capillary and serum glucose, serum β-hydroxybutyrate, urine dip (ketones), electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, phosphate, magnesium), urea/creatinine, CBC, osmolality, lactate, lipase, troponin, β-hCG (women of childbearing age), ECG (look for hyperkalemia / MI), CXR, urinalysis and cultures as indicated by source workup.
  • Calculate: anion gap, corrected Na⁺ (add 2.4 mmol/L per 5.5 mmol/L glucose > 5.5), effective osmolality (2 × Na⁺ + glucose).

3. Management Algorithm (Diabetes Canada 2018)

  1. Step 1 — Fluids (start immediately):
    • 0.9% NaCl 500–1000 mL IV bolus in the first hour (up to 15–20 mL/kg/h) to restore intravascular volume.
    • After the first hour, change to 0.9% NaCl or 0.45% NaCl at 250–500 mL/h, depending on corrected sodium: if corrected Na⁺ is normal or high, switch to 0.45% NaCl; if low, continue 0.9% NaCl.
    • Balanced crystalloid (Ringer's Lactate / Plasma-Lyte) may reduce hyperchloremic acidosis — consider after initial resuscitation (evidence: PLUS-DKA, SALT-ED subgroup).
    • Typical total deficit: 100 mL/kg (6–8 L); replace over 24–48 h. Avoid rapid correction in pediatric/adolescent patients (cerebral edema risk) — use 10 mL/kg bolus and replace deficit over 48 h.
  2. Step 2 — Potassium (mandatory before starting insulin):
    • Total body K⁺ is depleted (∼ 3–5 mmol/kg) even when serum K⁺ appears normal/high (acidosis shifts K⁺ extracellularly).
    • K⁺ < 3.3 mmol/L: HOLD insulin; give Potassium Chloride 20–40 mmol/h IV (peripheral max 10 mmol/h per line; central max 20 mmol/h) until K⁺ ≥ 3.3.
    • K⁺ 3.3–5.0 mmol/L: start insulin AND add Potassium Chloride 20–40 mmol to each litre of IV fluid (target K⁺ 4–5 mmol/L).
    • K⁺ > 5.0 mmol/L: start insulin, hold potassium; recheck q2h.
    • Requires ECG monitoring and telemetry; recheck K⁺ q2h initially, then q4h.
  3. Step 3 — Insulin (after fluid & K⁺ assessed):
    • Regular Insulin 0.1 units/kg IV bolus (optional — Diabetes Canada states bolus is not mandatory), then continuous infusion at 0.1 units/kg/h.
    • Alternative: 0.14 units/kg/h infusion without a bolus.
    • Expect glucose to fall 3–4 mmol/L/h; if not, double the infusion rate. Do not lower blood glucose faster than 5 mmol/L/h (cerebral edema risk).
    • Continue the IV infusion until anion gap closes (not just until glucose normalizes) — this is the critical end-point.
    • If patient uses a long-acting basal insulin at home (e.g., glargine, detemir, degludec), continue it to reduce rebound DKA on discontinuation of the IV infusion.
  4. Step 4 — Dextrose (glucose safety net):
    • When glucose reaches 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL), add Dextrose to the IV fluid — change to D5 ½ NS (or D10 ½ NS if still falling rapidly) to maintain glucose 10–14 mmol/L while continuing insulin to close the anion gap.
    • Do not decrease the insulin rate below 0.05 units/kg/h while anion gap remains elevated.
  5. Step 5 — Bicarbonate (rarely indicated):
    • Consider Sodium Bicarbonate only if pH < 7.00 after initial fluids, or for life-threatening hyperkalemia.
    • Typical dose: 100 mmol in 400 mL sterile water + 20 mmol KCl infused over 2 h; recheck pH q1h.
    • Routine bicarbonate is not recommended — may worsen cerebral edema, cause paradoxical CNS acidosis, and delay ketone clearance.
  6. Step 6 — Phosphate & Magnesium:
    • Phosphate < 0.3 mmol/L with symptoms (muscle weakness, respiratory failure, cardiac dysfunction) — replace with potassium phosphate 20–30 mmol IV.
    • Replace Mg²⁺ to ≥ 0.8 mmol/L (give Magnesium Sulfate 2 g IV over 15 min for symptomatic hypomagnesemia).

4. Monitoring Schedule

  • Hourly: vitals, glucose, fluid balance, neuro status (GCS — cerebral edema risk especially pediatric).
  • q2h: electrolytes, VBG (pH, HCO₃⁻), anion gap, serum ketones (β-OHB) until anion gap < 12.
  • Cardiac monitoring throughout; ICU/HDU admission for severe DKA, hemodynamic instability, or age < 21.

5. Resolution Criteria & Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin

  • DKA resolved: glucose < 11 mmol/L, HCO₃⁻ ≥ 18 mmol/L, venous pH > 7.30, anion gap ≤ 12.
  • Transition when resolved AND the patient is tolerating oral intake: overlap SC basal-bolus insulin (e.g., glargine 0.25 units/kg) with IV infusion for 2–4 hours before stopping IV insulin to prevent rebound DKA.
  • Involve Diabetes Education Service before discharge; ensure follow-up with endocrinology or primary care within 1–2 weeks; screen for precipitants and review sick-day management, glucagon access, and ketone monitoring.

6. Special Considerations

  • Euglycemic DKA: glucose < 14 mmol/L despite acidosis/ketosis — classic in SGLT2 inhibitor use (hold inhibitor, add Dextrose earlier), pregnancy, fasting, heavy alcohol use.
  • Pregnancy: lower diagnostic glucose threshold; obstetric/MFM consult; watch for fetal compromise; D5 containing fluid earlier.
  • Cerebral edema (predominantly pediatric; mortality ∼ 20–40%): headache, bradycardia + hypertension, altered GCS, seizure, incontinence. Treat with Mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV over 20 min or 3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg IV; elevate HOB; reduce IV rate by ∼ 1/3.
  • Infection: obtain cultures and empirically treat if suspected (fever may be absent in DKA even with significant infection).
Essential Emergency Laboratory Values

Clinical Indication

Standard Canadian SI-unit reference ranges for acute-care diagnostics and monitoring. Always correlate with the local laboratory report — reference intervals and critical-value cut-offs are assay- and institution-specific.

Electrolytes & Renal

  • Sodium (Na⁺): 135–145 mmol/L. Critical: < 120 or > 160. Correct severe hyponatremia at ≤ 8–10 mmol/L per 24 h to avoid osmotic demyelination; acute severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures, coma): 3% NaCl 150 mL IV over 10 min x up to 3.
  • Potassium (K⁺): 3.5–5.0 mmol/L. Critical: < 2.5 or > 6.0. Hyperkalemia ≥ 6.0 with ECG changes → Calcium Gluconate 10% 10–30 mL IV over 2–5 min (or Calcium Chloride 10% 5–10 mL via central line), then Regular Insulin 10 units IV + Dextrose 25–50 g IV, Salbutamol 10–20 mg nebulized, ± Sodium Bicarbonate if acidotic, and definitive removal (resin, diuresis, dialysis).
  • Chloride (Cl⁻): 98–106 mmol/L. Critical: < 80 or > 115.
  • Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻): 22–26 mmol/L. Critical: < 10 or > 40.
  • Anion Gap: 8–12 mmol/L (Na⁺ − [Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻]). Elevated AG (MUDPILES): Methanol, Uremia, DKA/alcoholic KA, Propylene glycol/Paraldehyde, Iron/INH, Lactate, Ethylene glycol, Salicylates.
  • Urea / BUN: 2.5–7.1 mmol/L (7–20 mg/dL). Critical: > 35 mmol/L.
  • Creatinine: Male 64–110 μmol/L; Female 53–97 μmol/L (0.6–1.2 mg/dL). Critical: > 650 μmol/L or an acute rise ≥ 26.5 μmol/L in 48 h (KDIGO AKI Stage 1).
  • Calcium (total): 2.15–2.55 mmol/L; correct for albumin (add 0.02 per 1 g/L albumin below 40). Ionized Ca²⁺: 1.12–1.32 mmol/L. Critical: < 0.8 or > 1.55 mmol/L ionized.
  • Magnesium: 0.70–1.05 mmol/L. Replace if < 0.8 with Magnesium Sulfate.
  • Phosphate: 0.80–1.45 mmol/L. Critical: < 0.3.

Glucose & Metabolic

  • Glucose: fasting 4.0–6.0; random < 11.1 mmol/L (< 7.8 if fasting). Critical: < 2.2 or > 22.2 mmol/L. Hypoglycemia < 4.0 mmol/L (Diabetes Canada): treat with oral glucose (15 g fast-acting carbohydrate) or Dextrose 25 g IV (50 mL D50W) if unable to take PO or altered LOC. Add Glucagon 1 mg IM/SC if no IV access; give Thiamine 100 mg IV first if chronic alcohol use/malnutrition.
  • Lactate: venous 0.5–2.2 mmol/L. ≥ 4 mmol/L defines severe hyperlactatemia; marker of tissue hypoperfusion/sepsis — triggers Surviving Sepsis 1-hour bundle.
  • β-hydroxybutyrate: < 0.6 mmol/L normal; ≥ 3.0 mmol/L diagnostic of DKA.
  • Osmolality: 275–295 mOsm/kg. Effective osmolality = 2 × Na⁺ + glucose.
  • Ammonia: 11–32 μmol/L; elevated in hepatic encephalopathy, urea-cycle disorders.

Hematology

  • Hemoglobin (Hb): Male 130–180 g/L; Female 120–160 g/L. Critical: < 70 or > 210 g/L. Transfusion threshold: < 70 g/L in most hospitalized adults; < 80 g/L in cardiovascular disease or symptomatic anemia (Canadian Blood Services / Choosing Wisely Canada).
  • Hematocrit: Male 0.40–0.54; Female 0.36–0.48.
  • WBC: 4.0–11.0 × 10⁹/L. Critical: < 2.0 or > 30.0. Absolute neutrophil count (ANC) < 0.5 × 10⁹/L = febrile neutropenia — antibiotics within 1 hour (e.g., Piperacillin/Tazobactam).
  • Platelets: 150–400 × 10⁹/L. Critical: < 20 (spontaneous bleeding risk) or > 1000. Transfusion threshold < 10 prophylactically, < 20 with fever/sepsis, < 50 with active bleeding or pre-procedure, < 100 for CNS bleed / neurosurgery.
  • INR: therapeutic on warfarin 2.0–3.0 (AF, VTE) or 2.5–3.5 (mechanical mitral valve). Reverse major bleeding on warfarin: IV Vitamin K 10 mg + PCC (Octaplex/Beriplex) 25–50 units/kg per Thrombosis Canada protocol.
  • aPTT: 25–35 s; therapeutic on heparin 1.5–2.5 × control. Reverse UFH with Protamine 1 mg per 100 units of heparin (max 50 mg).
  • Fibrinogen: 2.0–4.0 g/L. Replace with cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate if < 1.5 g/L in active bleeding / obstetric hemorrhage (target > 2 g/L in PPH).
  • D-dimer: < 500 ng/mL (or age-adjusted < age × 10 ng/mL if > 50 yr); negative helps rule out VTE in low-pretest-probability patients.

Cardiac / Inflammatory / Enzyme Markers

  • High-sensitivity Troponin (hs-TnT or hs-TnI): assay-specific cut-offs (99th percentile URL typically ~14 ng/L for hs-TnT, ~26–34 ng/L for hs-TnI). Canadian 0/1-h and 0/2-h pathways (CCS/CAEP): rule out at low baseline with low delta; rule in with elevated baseline or significant delta.
  • NT-proBNP: < 300 ng/L rules out acute heart failure in most patients (age-adjusted thresholds apply).
  • CK: Male 55–170 U/L; Female 30–135 U/L. Rhabdomyolysis ≥ 5 × ULN; target urine output 1–2 mL/kg/h with isotonic crystalloid.
  • Lipase: < 160 U/L; ≥ 3 × ULN in acute pancreatitis (Atlanta criteria).
  • AST / ALT: 10–40 U/L; > 1000 in ischemic/viral hepatitis, acetaminophen toxicity.
  • CRP: < 8 mg/L normal; elevations support infection/inflammation but non-specific.
  • Procalcitonin: < 0.1 μg/L — bacterial infection unlikely; > 0.5 suggests bacterial; > 2 strongly bacterial/septic.

Arterial / Venous Blood Gas

  • pH (arterial): 7.35–7.45 (venous ∼ 0.03–0.05 lower).
  • PaCO₂: 35–45 mmHg (4.7–6.0 kPa).
  • PaO₂: 80–100 mmHg on room air; P/F ratio < 300 = ARDS (Berlin criteria — mild 200–300, moderate 100–200, severe < 100).
  • Base Excess: −2 to +2.
  • Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb): < 3% non-smoker; < 10% smoker. > 25% or any symptoms → 100% O₂ ± HBO.
  • Methemoglobin: < 1%; > 20% causes cyanosis; treat with methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg IV if > 30% or symptomatic.

Toxicology / Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

  • Acetaminophen: treat per Rumack-Matthew nomogram (4-h level ≥ 150 mg/L) with NAC (21-h IV protocol: 150 mg/kg over 60 min, 50 mg/kg over 4 h, 100 mg/kg over 16 h).
  • Salicylate: toxic > 300 mg/L (2.2 mmol/L); alkalinize urine with Sodium Bicarbonate; hemodialysis if > 700 mg/L, altered LOC, or pulmonary edema.
  • Ethanol: legal limit 17.4 mmol/L (0.08 g/dL); anticipate withdrawal after clearance in chronic users.
  • Digoxin: therapeutic 0.6–2.0 nmol/L.
  • Lithium: therapeutic 0.6–1.2 mmol/L; > 2.5 mmol/L = severe toxicity (dialysis).
  • Phenytoin: total 10–20 mg/L (free 1–2 mg/L); correct for albumin.

Urinalysis

  • Positive nitrites + leukocyte esterase → UTI likely.
  • Dipstick blood positive with no RBC on microscopy → consider rhabdomyolysis / hemoglobinuria.
  • Ketones positive → DKA, AKA, starvation, SGLT2 inhibitor use.

Pregnancy

  • β-hCG (serum): positive > 5 IU/L. Discriminatory zone for transvaginal US of intrauterine pregnancy: 1500–2000 IU/L. Obtain in all women of reproductive age with abdominal pain or before imaging/RSI.

Adult Medicine Guidelines for Fever & Sepsis

Clinical Indication

Core temperature > 38.0 °C (100.4 °F) with suspected infectious etiology, or suspected sepsis/septic shock regardless of temperature (elderly and immunocompromised patients may be normothermic or hypothermic). Reference: Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021, AMMI Canada (Association of Medical Microbiology & Infectious Disease Canada), and the Canadian Critical Care Society.

1. Screening & Assessment

  • Sepsis screen: qSOFA ≥ 2 (altered mental status / GCS < 15, SBP ≤ 100 mmHg, RR ≥ 22/min) OR NEWS2 ≥ 5 — increasing Canadian adoption of NEWS2 over qSOFA for ward screening.
  • Septic shock criteria: Persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg AND serum lactate > 2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation (Sepsis-3).
  • Investigations (within first hour): Serum lactate, CBC, electrolytes, creatinine, liver enzymes, INR/PTT, venous/arterial blood gas, blood cultures × 2 (aerobic & anaerobic, from two separate sites before antibiotics if possible), urinalysis and culture, chest x-ray, and source-directed imaging (CT abdomen, lumbar puncture, soft-tissue ultrasound) as indicated.
  • Do not delay antibiotics > 1 hour in suspected septic shock or high-probability sepsis; in possible sepsis without shock, complete a rapid evaluation within 3 hours and treat if sepsis remains likely.

2. Hour-1 Resuscitation Bundle (Surviving Sepsis Campaign)

  • Measure lactate; remeasure within 2–4 h if initial > 2 mmol/L.
  • Obtain blood cultures prior to antibiotics (do not delay antibiotics to obtain).
  • Administer broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (see Section 3).
  • Begin balanced crystalloid (Ringer's lactate preferred over 0.9% NaCl — CCCTG/PLUS/BaSICS signal) at 30 mL/kg IV within the first 3 h for hypotension or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L. Reassess frequently (dynamic measures: passive leg raise, stroke volume variation, point-of-care ultrasound) to avoid fluid overload.
  • Start vasopressors for MAP < 65 mmHg during or after resuscitation.

3. Empiric Antibiotic Selection

  • Sepsis (unknown source): Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q24H PLUS Metronidazole 500 mg IV Q12H. If MRSA risk or unstable, add Vancomycin 25–30 mg/kg IV load.
  • Community-acquired pneumonia: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q24H PLUS Azithromycin 500 mg IV Q24H (covers atypicals). For severe CAP or ICU admission, consider Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV Q24H if beta-lactam allergy.
  • Urinary tract / pyelonephritis / urosepsis: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q24H. If ESBL risk, Piperacillin/Tazobactam 4.5 g IV Q6–8H.
  • Skin/soft-tissue (non-purulent cellulitis): Cefazolin 2 g IV Q8H (covers beta-haemolytic streptococci and MSSA).
  • Skin/soft-tissue (purulent or MRSA suspected): Clindamycin 600–900 mg IV Q8H, or Vancomycin. For necrotizing fasciitis add Piperacillin/Tazobactam + Clindamycin (toxin suppression) ± Vancomycin and seek urgent surgical debridement.
  • Intra-abdominal / hepatobiliary: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q24H PLUS Metronidazole 500 mg IV Q12H; alternative Piperacillin/Tazobactam 4.5 g IV Q6–8H for healthcare-associated or severe disease.
  • Suspected meningitis: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q12H + Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV (given with or before first antibiotic dose) ± Vancomycin + Ampicillin 2 g IV Q4H if > 50 y or immunocompromised (Listeria coverage).
  • Neutropenic fever: Piperacillin/Tazobactam 4.5 g IV Q6H or Cefepime 2 g IV Q8H (AMMI/IDSA).
  • Severe beta-lactam allergy: Consider Moxifloxacin or Ciprofloxacin + Metronidazole + Vancomycin per source.

4. Hemodynamic Support & Adjuncts

  • First-line vasopressor: Norepinephrine 0.05–0.5 mcg/kg/min IV titrated to MAP ≥ 65 mmHg (preferred per Surviving Sepsis Campaign).
  • Second-line / refractory shock: Add Vasopressin 0.03 units/min IV fixed-dose (norepinephrine-sparing); if still hypotensive, add Epinephrine infusion.
  • Stress-dose corticosteroids: Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day IV (50 mg Q6H or infusion) for vasopressor-dependent septic shock.
  • Glycemic control: Target blood glucose < 10 mmol/L (180 mg/dL) with Regular insulin infusion if persistently elevated.
  • Source control: Drain abscesses, remove infected lines/catheters, and obtain surgical/interventional consultation as soon as clinically feasible (ideally within 6–12 h).

5. Antipyretic & Supportive Care

  • Acetaminophen 650–1000 mg PO/IV Q6H PRN for patient comfort (max 4 g/day; reduce to 2 g/day if hepatic dysfunction or chronic alcohol use).
  • VTE prophylaxis with Enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily (or unfractionated heparin 5000 units SC Q8–12H if renal impairment or high bleed risk).
  • Stress ulcer prophylaxis with Pantoprazole 40 mg IV daily for patients with mechanical ventilation or coagulopathy.
  • Reassess antibiotic spectrum at 48–72 h (Choosing Wisely Canada: de-escalate to targeted therapy once cultures and sensitivities return).
Submersion Injury Management Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Respiratory impairment following submersion/immersion in a liquid medium. Current terminology (WHO / International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation / Lifesaving Society Canada / Canadian Drowning Prevention Coalition) classifies all events as "drowning" with outcomes of death, morbidity, or no morbidity. The terms "near-drowning", "wet", "dry", "secondary", or "silent" drowning are obsolete and should not be used.

1. Primary Resuscitation (Scene / ED)

  • Safely remove the patient from the water; rescuer safety first. In-water ventilation by trained rescuers may be considered for unresponsive patients in deep water when immediate extraction is not possible.
  • Chest compressions take priority after 2 rescue breaths — drowning is a hypoxic arrest, so initiate airway and breathing first, then follow standard BLS/ACLS (Heart & Stroke Foundation of Canada 2020 update, reaffirmed 2024). Avoid abdominal thrusts to expel water — ineffective and increases aspiration risk.
  • C-spine: Routine in-line stabilization is not recommended and may delay resuscitation. Reserve for clear history of shallow-water diving, high-impact trauma, intoxication, or focal neurologic deficit.
  • Initiate AED as soon as available; dry the chest before applying pads. Continue CPR with standard compression-to-ventilation ratios.
  • All symptomatic drowning patients, or those with history of submersion > 1 min, altered LOC, hypoxia, or any CPR performed, require ED transport and a minimum 4–8 h observation (delayed pulmonary edema can develop).

2. Airway & Breathing

  • Administer 100% high-flow oxygen by non-rebreather or high-flow nasal cannula; titrate to SpO₂ 94–98% once stable.
  • Consider non-invasive ventilation (CPAP 5–10 cmH₂O) for cooperative patients with moderate respiratory distress but preserved mental status.
  • Indications for Rapid Sequence Intubation (see RSI algorithm): inability to protect airway, GCS ≤ 8, severe respiratory distress, or persistent SpO₂ < 90% on high-flow O₂.
  • Lung-protective ventilation: Vᴹ 6–8 mL/kg ideal body weight, PEEP 5–15 cmH₂O titrated to oxygenation (surfactant washout causes ARDS-like physiology), plateau pressure < 30 cmH₂O.
  • Do not give prophylactic antibiotics or corticosteroids — no benefit and may promote resistant infection. Treat infection only if clinical/radiographic/microbiologic evidence emerges (highly contaminated water: consider Piperacillin/Tazobactam).

3. Circulation & Neuro

  • Establish IV/IO access; correct hypovolemia with balanced crystalloid (Ringer's lactate).
  • Treat dysrhythmias per ACLS; bradyarrhythmias are common with hypothermia/hypoxia.
  • Check point-of-care glucose and correct hypoglycemia with Dextrose (D50W) 25–50 mL IV.
  • Post-ROSC: targeted temperature management 32–36 °C for 24 h in comatose survivors (H&SF/ILCOR); avoid hyperthermia.

4. Temperature Management (Hypothermia)

  • Obtain continuous core temperature (esophageal probe preferred in intubated patients; bladder or rectal acceptable).
  • Remove wet clothing, dry the patient, and initiate passive (warm blankets) and active external rewarming (forced-air warmer / Bair Hugger). Use warmed IV fluids (40–42 °C) and humidified warm oxygen.
  • Severe hypothermia (core < 30 °C) with cardiac arrest: limit defibrillation to 3 attempts and withhold IV resuscitation medications until core ≥ 30 °C; space subsequent drug doses at double the usual interval between 30–34 °C. Continue CPR until rewarmed (“not dead until warm and dead” — BC & Canadian Hypothermia CPG).
  • Consider extracorporeal rewarming (ECMO, CRRT, cardiopulmonary bypass) for refractory arrest, core < 28 °C, or hemodynamic instability — early notification of a receiving ECLS-capable centre is recommended.

5. Disposition

  • Asymptomatic with normal exam, SpO₂, CXR, and > 6 h observation: discharge with return-precautions.
  • Any symptoms, abnormal imaging, or required O₂ support: admit for monitoring and serial exams.
Hypertensive Emergencies Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Severe hypertension (SBP > 180 mmHg or DBP > 120 mmHg) with new or progressive target-organ dysfunction (hypertensive emergency). Severe BP elevation without end-organ injury is a hypertensive urgency — treat over hours to days with oral agents; avoid rapid IV reduction. References: Hypertension Canada 2020 Guidelines (reaffirmed 2024), Canadian Cardiovascular Society, and Canadian Stroke Best Practice Recommendations 2022.

1. Assessment

  • Confirm BP with a properly sized cuff in both arms (> 20 mmHg interarm difference ⇒ suspect aortic dissection).
  • Target organ screen: neurologic exam (stroke, encephalopathy, seizures), fundoscopy (papilledema, hemorrhages), cardiopulmonary (chest pain, pulmonary edema, new murmur), renal (urinalysis, creatinine), and obstetric (preeclampsia / eclampsia if pregnant or < 6 wk postpartum).
  • Investigations: 12-lead ECG, troponin, CBC, electrolytes, creatinine, urinalysis, and chest x-ray; CT/CTA head & chest/abdomen/pelvis as guided by presentation.
  • Establish IV access, continuous cardiac & BP monitoring (arterial line preferred for titratable infusions).

2. Condition-Specific BP Targets

  • Aortic dissection: SBP < 120 mmHg and HR < 60 bpm within 20 min (lower dP/dt with beta-blocker before vasodilator to avoid reflex tachycardia).
  • Acute ischemic stroke (not for thrombolysis): Do not lower BP unless > 220/120 mmHg; then reduce ≤ 15% in first 24 h (Canadian Stroke Best Practices).
  • Acute ischemic stroke (candidate for thrombolysis / EVT): Reduce and maintain BP ≤ 185/110 mmHg before and ≤ 180/105 mmHg for 24 h after Alteplase/Tenecteplase.
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH): Acute smooth reduction to SBP 140 mmHg (INTERACT-2 / Canadian Stroke); avoid < 130 mmHg.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: SBP < 160 mmHg pre-aneurysm securement.
  • Acute coronary syndrome / pulmonary edema: Reduce MAP 10–20% in the first hour while relieving ischemia / preload.
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy: Reduce MAP 20–25% over the first hour, then slowly to 160/100 over 2–6 h.
  • Severe preeclampsia / eclampsia: Target SBP < 155 mmHg and DBP < 105 mmHg (SOGC 2022); simultaneous seizure prophylaxis with Magnesium sulfate 4–6 g IV load over 15–20 min then 1–2 g/h infusion.
  • Pheochromocytoma / sympathetic crisis (cocaine, MAOI): Use alpha-blockade or combined agents (phentolamine, nitroglycerin); avoid beta-blocker monotherapy (unopposed alpha).

3. Pharmacologic Management (IV Agents Available in Canada)

  • Labetalol (alpha/beta blocker — first-line for most emergencies): 10–20 mg IV over 1–2 min, may repeat or double every 10 min to max cumulative 300 mg; or infusion 0.5–2 mg/min. Avoid in severe asthma/COPD, 2nd/3rd-degree AV block, decompensated heart failure, or HR < 50.
  • Nitroglycerin (venodilator): First-line for ACS, acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema, or flash pulmonary edema. IV infusion 10–20 mcg/min, titrate by 10 mcg/min every 3–5 min; usual range 40–200 mcg/min. Avoid with PDE-5 inhibitor use (≤ 24–48 h), RV infarct, or severe AS.
  • Esmolol (short-acting cardioselective beta-blocker): Preferred for aortic dissection rate control. 500 mcg/kg IV bolus over 1 min, then 50–200 mcg/kg/min infusion. (Not presently in this reference's medication list.)
  • Hydralazine (direct arteriolar vasodilator): 5–10 mg IV Q20–30 min PRN — second-line; preferred option in severe preeclampsia when labetalol contraindicated. Unpredictable response and reflex tachycardia; avoid in dissection or ischemic heart disease.
  • Nicardipine / Clevidipine (dihydropyridine CCB infusions): Where available, excellent titratability for neurologic emergencies (Canadian Stroke Best Practices endorse nicardipine 5–15 mg/h).
  • Sodium nitroprusside: 0.25–10 mcg/kg/min; reserve for refractory emergencies — monitor for cyanide toxicity (> 24 h, high dose, renal or hepatic impairment). Antidote: Sodium thiosulfate.
  • Furosemide: Furosemide 40–80 mg IV for volume-overloaded patients with pulmonary edema in addition to vasodilator therapy.

4. Pitfalls

  • Avoid sublingual nifedipine (unpredictable precipitous drops → stroke/MI).
  • Avoid abrupt > 25% MAP reduction in the first hour (except dissection / preeclampsia / ICH targets); autoregulation is impaired and can precipitate watershed ischemia.
  • Search for secondary cause (renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma, primary aldosteronism, stimulant use, thyroid storm, withdrawal of clonidine/beta-blocker).
Alcohol-Related Emergencies Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Acute alcohol intoxication, alcohol withdrawal syndrome (AWS), delirium tremens (DT), Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, or alcoholic ketoacidosis. References: CIHR / Canadian Coalition for Seniors' Mental Health AWS Guideline, CRISM (Canadian Research Initiative in Substance Misuse) 2023, and Canada's Low-Risk Alcohol Drinking Guidelines (CCSA).

1. Primary Assessment

  • Assess ABCs; protect airway (lateral recovery position if vomiting or obtunded). Monitor for aspiration — consider RSI for GCS ≤ 8 or absent gag reflex.
  • Establish IV access. Obtain point-of-care capillary blood glucose immediately.
  • Investigations: CBC, electrolytes (including Mg, PO₄, Ca), BUN/creatinine, liver enzymes, INR, lipase, venous/arterial blood gas with lactate, serum osmolality & osmolar gap (rule out toxic alcohols), ethanol level, acetaminophen/salicylate co-ingestion screen, ECG, and beta-hCG in female patients.
  • Screen for head injury (CT if trauma, anticoagulant, or focal neurologic deficit), GI bleeding, pancreatitis, hepatic decompensation, and infection (low threshold for sepsis workup).
  • Always give Thiamine BEFORE dextrose in malnourished / chronic-use patients to prevent precipitation of Wernicke's encephalopathy (classic triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion).

2. Withdrawal Severity Stratification (CIWA-Ar)

  • CIWA-Ar < 10: mild; supportive care + scheduled/PRN oral benzodiazepine.
  • CIWA-Ar 10–19: moderate; symptom-triggered IV benzodiazepines, close monitoring.
  • CIWA-Ar ≥ 20 or history of DTs / withdrawal seizure: severe — monitored bed, consider ICU.
  • Complicated withdrawal: seizures within 6–48 h of last drink; DTs (autonomic instability, hallucinations, delirium) within 48–96 h.

3. Medication Protocol

  • Thiamine: 500 mg IV TID × 3 days for suspected / confirmed Wernicke's encephalopathy (CRISM 2023); otherwise 100–300 mg IV/IM daily × 3–5 days, then oral. Continue for any malnourished or chronic-use patient.
  • Multivitamin "Banana Bag": 1 ampoule MVI + 1 g Magnesium sulfate + folate 1 mg in 1 L balanced crystalloid over 2–4 h; correct hypoglycemia, hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia, and hypophosphatemia (refeeding risk).
  • Benzodiazepines (first-line for AWS, symptom-triggered):
    • Diazepam 5–10 mg IV Q5–10 min until CIWA < 10 or sedation achieved (long half-life provides self-tapering).
    • Lorazepam 1–4 mg IV/IM Q15–20 min PRN — preferred for hepatic dysfunction or elderly (no active metabolites).
    • Midazolam 2–5 mg IV/IM Q5–10 min PRN when IV access delayed.
  • Refractory DT / benzodiazepine-resistant withdrawal: Phenobarbital (adjunct or alternative) 10 mg/kg IV load; or Propofol infusion 20–80 mcg/kg/min (requires intubation); Ketamine 0.3 mg/kg IV load then 0.3 mg/kg/h as benzodiazepine-sparing adjunct. Consult ICU.
  • Agitation / psychosis: Haloperidol 2.5–5 mg IV/IM (second-line; monitor ECG for QTc prolongation, and caution — lowers seizure threshold). Combine with benzodiazepine; do not use haloperidol as sole agent in AWS.
  • Withdrawal seizures: Lorazepam 2–4 mg IV or Diazepam 5–10 mg IV; refractory → see Seizures algorithm.
  • Hypoglycemia: Dextrose (D50W) 25–50 mL IV (after thiamine).
  • Nausea/vomiting: Ondansetron 4–8 mg IV (check QTc) or Metoclopramide 10 mg IV.

4. Disposition & Harm Reduction

  • Admit for complicated withdrawal, DTs, concurrent trauma/infection/GI bleed, or inability to tolerate PO.
  • Offer evidence-based addiction care: brief intervention, naltrexone or acamprosate, referral to CRISM-recommended outpatient/community programs, and linkage to Indigenous-led or culturally appropriate services where applicable.
  • Screen and treat concurrent substance use and mental health conditions; provide naloxone take-home kits if opioid co-use is suspected.
Acute Pelvic Pain Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Acute onset lower abdominal / pelvic pain in a person with a uterus or ovaries. References: Society of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists of Canada (SOGC) Clinical Practice Guidelines; Association of Medical Microbiology and Infectious Disease Canada (AMMI / Public Health Agency of Canada STBBI 2023 guidance); Canadian Association of Emergency Physicians (CAEP) acute pelvic pain resource.

1. Diagnostic Priority — Rule Out Life/Fertility-Threatening Causes

  • Always obtain urine or quantitative serum beta-hCG on any patient of reproductive age (including post-tubal-ligation and those using contraception).
  • Cannot miss diagnoses: ruptured ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, tubo-ovarian abscess, hemorrhagic ovarian cyst, appendicitis, PID with perihepatitis (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis), and placental abruption / uterine rupture if > 20 wk gestation.
  • Investigations: CBC, beta-hCG (quantitative if positive), blood type & Rh with antibody screen, urinalysis & culture, NAAT for gonorrhea/chlamydia/trichomonas (vaginal/endocervical), lactate if septic, and transvaginal/transabdominal ultrasound (gold standard for ectopic, torsion, TOA — Doppler flow).
  • Point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) to assess for intrauterine pregnancy, free fluid in Morison's/cul-de-sac, and gross adnexal pathology.

2. Hemodynamic Assessment & Resuscitation

  • Unstable (SBP < 90, HR > 110, syncope, peritonitis, positive FAST): 2 large-bore IVs (16–18 G), balanced crystalloid bolus, type & crossmatch 2–4 units PRBCs, activate massive transfusion protocol for suspected ruptured ectopic with Class III/IV shock (see Hemorrhagic Shock Blood Protocol). Urgent OB/GYN and general surgery consultation.
  • Stable: Pelvic / bimanual exam with speculum, wet mount, NAAT swabs; directed imaging.

3. Differential-Directed Management

  • Ectopic pregnancy: Ruptured → surgical management. Unruptured, stable, beta-hCG < 5000 IU/L, mass < 3.5 cm, no cardiac activity: methotrexate 50 mg/m² IM single-dose (SOGC) with follow-up beta-hCG day 4 & 7.
  • Ovarian torsion: Surgical emergency — detorsion and cystectomy; diagnosis clinical + ultrasound (absent/abnormal Doppler flow is suggestive but not required). Do not delay surgery for imaging if high suspicion.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (outpatient / mild): Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM × 1 + Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID × 14 days + Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID × 14 days (PHAC STBBI 2023 — ceftriaxone dose increased from 250 to 500 mg due to gonococcal resistance).
  • PID (inpatient / severe / TOA / pregnant / failed outpatient): Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q24H + Doxycycline 100 mg PO/IV Q12H + Metronidazole 500 mg IV Q12H; alternatively Cefoxitin 2 g IV Q6H + Doxycycline. Admit all TOAs; surgical drainage if > 7 cm or fails 48–72 h medical therapy.
  • Appendicitis: Surgical consultation; preoperative antibiotics Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole or Piperacillin/Tazobactam.
  • Hemorrhagic ovarian cyst: Most resolve with supportive care and analgesia; surgical intervention for ongoing bleeding or instability.

4. Analgesia & Symptom Control

  • Acetaminophen 650–1000 mg PO/IV Q6H (max 4 g/day).
  • Ketorolac 10–15 mg IV/IM Q6H (avoid if pregnant, renal dysfunction, active GI bleeding).
  • Hydromorphone 0.5–1 mg IV Q2–4H PRN or Morphine 2–4 mg IV Q2–4H PRN for severe pain.
  • Ondansetron 4–8 mg IV for nausea; Dimenhydrinate 25–50 mg IV as alternative.
  • Hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan) 10–20 mg IV/IM for visceral colic.

5. Special Considerations

  • Administer Rh immunoglobulin (RhoGAM 300 mcg IM/IV) within 72 h for Rh-negative patients with bleeding, ectopic, or trauma in pregnancy (SOGC).
  • Report sexual assault sensitively; offer forensic examination, STBBI prophylaxis, emergency contraception, and social work / victim services referral.
  • Partner notification and treatment for confirmed STBBI (public health reporting).
Vaginal Bleeding Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB), heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), bleeding in early pregnancy (< 20 wk), antepartum hemorrhage (≥ 20 wk), or postpartum hemorrhage (PPH). References: SOGC Clinical Practice Guidelines (HMB 2022; PPH 2022; Early Pregnancy Loss 2023); Canadian Blood Services Massive Hemorrhage Protocol.

1. Primary Assessment

  • ABCs, full vitals, and shock index (HR/SBP > 1 = hemodynamic compromise). Estimate blood loss (count pads/clots, weigh perineal drapes).
  • Focused history: LMP, contraception, pregnancy status, gestational age, parity, bleeding duration & volume, associated pain, trauma, coagulopathy (personal or family), medications (anticoagulants, antiplatelets, hormonal therapy), and STBBI risk.
  • Pregnancy test on every patient of reproductive age. If positive, clarify gestational age and intrauterine location (ultrasound).
  • Investigations: CBC, blood type & Rh with antibody screen, quantitative beta-hCG (if pregnant or unknown), coagulation panel (INR/PTT, fibrinogen), electrolytes, creatinine, group & screen (crossmatch 2–4 U PRBC if unstable), TSH, ferritin (for HMB workup); pelvic ultrasound (transabdominal + transvaginal).

2. Etiology-Directed Workup

  • Non-pregnant AUB (PALM-COEIN framework): structural (Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia) vs non-structural (Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not-yet-classified).
  • Early pregnancy (< 20 wk): threatened / inevitable / incomplete / complete / missed miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, gestational trophoblastic disease.
  • Late pregnancy (≥ 20 wk): placental abruption (painful, rigid uterus, +/- concealed hemorrhage), placenta previa (painless), vasa previa, uterine rupture, bloody show.
  • Postpartum (< 24 h primary; 24 h–12 wk secondary): the 4 Ts — Tone (uterine atony, > 70% of cases), Trauma (lacerations, hematoma, inversion), Tissue (retained products, accreta spectrum), Thrombin (coagulopathy, DIC, amniotic fluid embolism).

3. Resuscitation (Unstable or Massive Hemorrhage)

  • Two large-bore IVs (16–18 G) or IO access; balanced crystalloid (Ringer's lactate) 1–2 L while activating Massive Hemorrhage Protocol (MHP).
  • Early 1:1:1 transfusion (PRBC : FFP : platelets); give uncrossmatched O Rh-negative blood if crossmatch not ready (see Hemorrhagic Shock algorithm).
  • Maintain normothermia, treat acidosis/hypocalcemia (1 g Calcium gluconate or Calcium chloride after every 4 units of blood).
  • Emergent OB/GYN, anesthesia, and interventional radiology consultation; early hysterectomy for uncontrolled PPH is lifesaving (do not delay).

4. Medication Protocol

  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): 1 g IV over 10 min within 3 h of onset of PPH (WOMAN trial; SOGC 2022). May repeat 1 g IV after 30 min if bleeding continues. Also effective in severe HMB (1–1.5 g PO TID for up to 5 days).
  • Uterotonics (PPH, sequential escalation):
    • Oxytocin 10 IU IM / 5 IU IV bolus, then 20–40 IU in 1 L crystalloid IV infusion (first-line; Canadian standard of care).
    • Carbetocin 100 mcg IV over 1 min (single dose; Canadian preferred agent after Cesarean and increasingly after vaginal delivery).
    • Ergonovine (Methergine) 0.2 mg IM Q2–4 h PRN (contraindicated in hypertension, preeclampsia, cardiac disease).
    • Carboprost (Hemabate) 250 mcg IM Q15 min (max 2 mg); avoid in asthma.
    • Misoprostol 800–1000 mcg PR (or 600 mcg SL) — off-label but SOGC-endorsed adjunct.
  • Rh prophylaxis: Rh immunoglobulin (WinRho/RhoGAM) 300 mcg IM/IV within 72 h for Rh-negative patients with bleeding in pregnancy (dose by Kleihauer-Betke if ≥ 20 wk). (SOGC)
  • Hormonal management (stable non-pregnant AUB): high-dose combined oral contraceptive, medroxyprogesterone 10–20 mg PO daily, or IV conjugated estrogen 25 mg Q4–6H.
  • Analgesia: Ketorolac 10–15 mg IV (also reduces menstrual flow), Morphine or Hydromorphone for severe pain.
  • Antibiotics: For septic abortion or endometritis — Ceftriaxone 2 g IV + Metronidazole 500 mg IV + Doxycycline or Clindamycin 900 mg IV + Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV.

5. Mechanical / Surgical Adjuncts

  • Bimanual uterine massage / compression.
  • Intrauterine balloon tamponade (Bakri, Foley, condom-catheter) for refractory atony.
  • Non-pneumatic anti-shock garment for transport to definitive care.
  • Uterine artery embolization (interventional radiology) or surgical options: B-Lynch suture, internal iliac ligation, hysterectomy.
  • Evacuation of retained products of conception (D&C / manual vacuum aspiration).

6. Supportive & Follow-Up Care

  • Iron replacement for symptomatic anemia (oral elemental iron 150–200 mg/day; IV iron sucrose / iron isomaltoside if severe or intolerant).
  • Psychosocial support and early pregnancy loss counseling as appropriate.
  • Document massive transfusion metrics (CCBS quality indicators) and debrief team.
Seizures Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Active generalized tonic-clonic (or focal with impaired awareness) seizure, or status epilepticus (SE) — defined as continuous seizure activity ≥ 5 min, or ≥ 2 discrete seizures without recovery of consciousness in between. References: Canadian League Against Epilepsy / Epilepsy Canada; Neurocritical Care Society Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Status Epilepticus; Canadian Association of Emergency Physicians (CAEP) position on SE; ESETT (Established Status Epilepticus Treatment Trial, 2019).

1. Primary Management (0–5 min)

  • Protect airway and head from injury — do not place objects in mouth or restrain.
  • Position left lateral decubitus; suction secretions; apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather (target SpO₂ ≥ 94%).
  • Time the seizure from onset (critical for pharmacologic decision-making). Apply cardiac monitor, SpO₂, capnography.
  • Establish IV access (IO if unable); draw CBC, electrolytes, glucose, calcium, magnesium, Cr, liver enzymes, venous blood gas + lactate, anticonvulsant levels (if on therapy), beta-hCG, toxicology screen. Obtain 12-lead ECG (rule out long-QT, Brugada).
  • Check point-of-care glucose: if < 4 mmol/L, give Dextrose (D50W) 25–50 mL IV; give Thiamine 100 mg IV first in malnourished / alcohol-use patients.
  • Eclampsia (pregnant > 20 wk or < 6 wk postpartum): Magnesium sulfate 4–6 g IV over 15–20 min, then 1–2 g/h infusion (SOGC).
  • Isoniazid-induced seizure: pyridoxine (vitamin B6) 1 g per gram of INH ingested (up to 5 g) IV.
  • Hyponatremic seizure: 3% saline 100 mL IV bolus, may repeat × 2.

2. First-Line Pharmacotherapy (Benzodiazepines, 5–20 min)

  • Midazolam: 10 mg IM (preferred if no IV, age ≥ 40 kg; RAMPART trial) OR 0.2 mg/kg IV/IN/buccal (max 10 mg). Repeat once after 5 min if seizure continues.
  • Lorazepam: 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg/dose); may repeat once at 5 min.
  • Diazepam: 0.15–0.2 mg/kg IV (max 10 mg/dose) or 0.2–0.5 mg/kg PR; may repeat in 5 min.
  • Under-dosing is the most common error — give full weight-based dose, even in "borderline" patients.

3. Second-Line Anti-Seizure Medication (20–40 min)

  • ESETT / NCS: all three agents below have equivalent efficacy — choice is guided by availability and contraindications.
  • Levetiracetam (Keppra): 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) over 10 min. Preferred in most Canadian centres (favourable safety profile; no hepatic/cardiac contraindications).
  • Fosphenytoin: 20 mg PE/kg IV at ≤ 150 mg PE/min (or Phenytoin 20 mg/kg IV at ≤ 50 mg/min — cardiac monitoring mandatory). Avoid with heart block, hypotension, or Brugada syndrome. See Phenytoin entry for infusion details.
  • Valproate: 40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg) over 10 min. Avoid in hepatic disease, pregnancy, suspected mitochondrial disease, or urea-cycle disorder. See Valproic acid entry.

4. Refractory Status Epilepticus (> 40 min or failed 2nd-line)

  • Intubate (RSI checklist); initiate continuous EEG monitoring as soon as available.
  • Continuous infusion of one of the following, titrated to burst-suppression or seizure cessation:
    • Midazolam: 0.2 mg/kg IV load, then 0.05–2 mg/kg/h infusion.
    • Propofol: 1–2 mg/kg IV load, then 30–200 mcg/kg/min infusion (monitor for PRIS — lactate, CK, triglycerides).
    • Ketamine: 0.5–5 mg/kg IV bolus, then 50–250 mg/h (0.5–5 mg/kg/h) infusion titrated to EEG burst suppression. Useful adjunct in super-refractory SE.
    • Pentobarbital: 5–15 mg/kg IV load, then 0.5–5 mg/kg/h (ICU; significant hypotension).
  • Maintain 24–48 h of seizure freedom then taper infusion while continuing maintenance anti-seizure medication.

5. Etiologic Workup & Disposition

  • Investigate underlying cause: CT head (urgent if first seizure, focal deficit, trauma, anticoagulant use, immunocompromised, or > 40 y), MRI + EEG as inpatient, lumbar puncture if meningitis/encephalitis suspected (start empiric Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin + Acyclovir before imaging if clinically urgent).
  • First unprovoked seizure: neurology follow-up; counsel on driving restrictions per provincial MVA regulations (typically 3–12 months seizure-free; physicians in most Canadian provinces have a legal duty to report).
  • Known epilepsy: verify medication adherence, drug levels; adjust regimen with neurology.
Universal Antidotes Algorithm

Clinical Indication

Suspected toxicological overdose with respiratory depression, altered mental status, cardiovascular collapse, seizures, or characteristic toxidrome. References: Canadian Association of Poison Control Centres (CAPCC), Poison & Drug Information Services (Ontario Poison Centre 1-800-268-9017 / IWK Regional Poison Centre / BC Drug & Poison Information Centre), Canadian Pharmacists Association Compendium of Therapeutics (CPS), and Choosing Wisely Canada toxicology guidance.

1. Primary Assessment & Resuscitation

  • Support ABCs; secure airway and provide positive-pressure ventilation if RR < 8/min, SpO₂ < 90%, or inability to protect airway. Have RSI equipment ready.
  • Continuous cardiac monitor, SpO₂, capnography, and full vital signs including core temperature.
  • Establish IV access (two sites if unstable). Obtain 12-lead ECG (QRS, QTc, R in aVR), point-of-care glucose, and baseline labs: CBC, electrolytes, extended electrolytes (Ca, Mg, PO₄), creatinine, liver enzymes, INR/PTT, VBG with lactate, osmolality (calculate osmolar gap), anion gap, salicylate & acetaminophen levels, ethanol, beta-hCG, and targeted drug levels.
  • Identify toxidrome: opioid, sedative-hypnotic, sympathomimetic, anticholinergic, cholinergic, serotonergic, NMS.
  • Call the regional Poison Centre early for decision support — 1-844-POISON-X connects to the provincial service; Ontario: 1-800-268-9017.
  • Decontamination: activated charcoal 1 g/kg (max 50 g) PO/NG within 1 h of ingestion in cooperative, airway-protected patients; whole-bowel irrigation with PEG for sustained-release or body-packer ingestions; avoid gastric lavage and forced emesis.

2. Specific Antidote Protocols

  • Opioid overdose: Naloxone 0.04–0.4 mg IV/IM/IN (start low in chronic users to avoid precipitated withdrawal), repeat Q2–3 min titrated to RR > 10 and adequate ventilation (not full consciousness). For long-acting opioids (methadone, fentanyl patches, sustained-release): infusion at 2/3 of effective bolus dose per hour. Distribute take-home naloxone kits at discharge (Canadian Opioid Response) and offer buprenorphine/naloxone induction if ongoing OUD.
  • Benzodiazepine overdose: Flumazenil 0.2 mg IV over 15 s, repeat 0.1 mg Q1 min PRN (max 1 mg). Contraindicated in chronic benzodiazepine use, co-ingestion with pro-convulsants (TCAs, bupropion), or seizure disorder — can precipitate status epilepticus. Generally reserved for iatrogenic oversedation.
  • Acetaminophen overdose: Plot 4-h post-ingestion level on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram. Give N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) IV: 150 mg/kg in 200 mL D5W over 60 min, then 50 mg/kg in 500 mL D5W over 4 h, then 100 mg/kg in 1 L D5W over 16 h (total 300 mg/kg over 21 h; Canadian 21-h Prescott protocol). Extend infusion for massive ingestion or rising ALT/INR.
  • Salicylate (ASA) overdose: Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus then 150 mEq in 1 L D5W at 1.5–2× maintenance (urine pH > 7.5; serum pH 7.45–7.55); replace potassium. Hemodialysis for level > 6.5 mmol/L (90 mg/dL), altered LOC, pulmonary edema, or renal failure.
  • Tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) overdose: QRS > 100 ms or ventricular dysrhythmia → Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus (may repeat to QRS < 100 ms), followed by bicarbonate infusion. Avoid Class Ia/Ic antiarrhythmics; seizures treated with benzodiazepines. Intralipid 20% for refractory cardiotoxicity.
  • Beta-blocker overdose: Glucagon 5–10 mg IV bolus, then 2–10 mg/h infusion. Add high-dose-insulin euglycemic therapy (HIET): Regular insulin 1 unit/kg IV bolus + Dextrose 0.5 g/kg, then insulin 1–10 units/kg/h with dextrose infusion titrated to euglycemia. Add Epinephrine or Dopamine for refractory shock; consider IV lipid emulsion, ECMO/VA-ECLS.
  • Calcium channel blocker overdose: Calcium gluconate 3 g IV or Calcium chloride 1 g IV Q10–20 min × 3–4 doses, then infusion. HIET as above. Vasopressors (Epinephrine, Vasopressin). Transvenous pacing if refractory bradycardia. IV lipid emulsion (20% intralipid 1.5 mL/kg bolus then 0.25 mL/kg/min) for cardiovascular collapse.
  • Cholinergic (organophosphate / nerve agent) toxicity: Atropine 2–5 mg IV, doubling every 3–5 min until bronchial secretions dry. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) 30 mg/kg IV over 30 min, then 8 mg/kg/h infusion (order from CBS National Emergency Stockpile). Decontaminate skin/clothing.
  • Sympathomimetic (cocaine, methamphetamine, MDMA): Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Midazolam, Diazepam) first-line for agitation/tachycardia/hypertension. Active cooling for hyperthermia. Avoid beta-blocker monotherapy (unopposed alpha). Nitroglycerin or phentolamine for cocaine-associated chest pain.
  • Methanol / ethylene glycol (toxic alcohols): Fomepizole 15 mg/kg IV load, then 10 mg/kg Q12H × 4, then 15 mg/kg Q12H (1st-line, Canadian availability via provincial stock). Ethanol infusion is an alternative if fomepizole unavailable (target level 22 mmol/L). Adjuncts: folate for methanol, thiamine + pyridoxine for ethylene glycol, Sodium bicarbonate for acidemia. Hemodialysis for pH < 7.25, end-organ injury, or high level.
  • Digoxin toxicity: Digoxin-specific Fab (DigiFab / DigiBind) for life-threatening arrhythmia, hyperkalemia > 5 mmol/L, level > 15 nmol/L, or hemodynamic instability. Do not give calcium for hyperkalemia in digoxin toxicity (theoretical "stone heart").
  • Iron overdose: Deferoxamine 15 mg/kg/h IV infusion for serum iron > 90 μmol/L (500 mcg/dL), metabolic acidosis, or shock.
  • Cyanide (e.g., smoke inhalation, nitroprusside): Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) 5 g IV over 15 min (preferred; Health Canada approved). Alternative: Sodium thiosulfate 12.5 g IV + sodium nitrite 300 mg IV.
  • Methemoglobinemia: Methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg IV over 5 min for MetHb > 20% or symptomatic. Avoid in G6PD deficiency.
  • Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST): Stop injection; 20% lipid emulsion 1.5 mL/kg bolus then 0.25 mL/kg/min infusion until circulatory stability.
  • Warfarin / vitamin K antagonist reversal: Vitamin K 5–10 mg IV slow + Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (Octaplex/Beriplex) per Canadian Blood Services protocol (weight- and INR-dosed). FFP only if PCC unavailable.
  • DOAC reversal: Dabigatran → idarucizumab (Praxbind) 5 g IV; apixaban/rivaroxaban → andexanet alfa (where available) or 4-factor PCC 25–50 U/kg IV.
  • Heparin: Protamine sulfate 1 mg per 100 units heparin given in the last 2–3 h (max 50 mg per dose).
  • Neuromuscular blockade reversal: Sugammadex for rocuronium/vecuronium (2–16 mg/kg IV per depth of block).
  • Malignant hyperthermia / neuroleptic malignant syndrome / serotonin syndrome: Discontinue trigger, aggressive cooling, benzodiazepines; dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV for MH; cyproheptadine 12 mg PO then 2 mg Q2H for serotonin syndrome; bromocriptine for NMS.

3. Observation & Disposition

  • Psychosocial assessment, suicide-risk evaluation, and safety planning for every intentional overdose (CAEP / Canadian Mental Health Association best practice).
  • Admit for ongoing symptoms, abnormal vital signs, persistent ECG changes, or delayed-release ingestions.
  • Referral to addiction medicine, harm-reduction services, and distribution of take-home naloxone kits where applicable.
  • Report mandated exposures (occupational, pediatric maltreatment, environmental) to public health / child welfare.
Hemorrhagic Shock Blood Protocol Adults

Clinical Indication

Suspected or confirmed massive hemorrhage with Class III/IV shock (tachycardia > 120, SBP < 90 mmHg, narrowed pulse pressure, altered mental status, cool/mottled skin, lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L, or base deficit > 6). References: Trauma Association of Canada (TAC); Canadian Blood Services National Advisory Committee on Blood and Blood Products (NAC) — Massive Hemorrhage Protocol (MHP) Framework 2022; Choosing Wisely Canada (Transfusion Medicine); CRASH-2 / CRASH-3 / PATCH-Trauma; ATLS 10th edition; STOP the Bleed Canada.

1. Identification & MHP Activation

  • Activation triggers (any of): ABC score ≥ 2 (penetrating mechanism, SBP ≤ 90, HR ≥ 120, positive FAST), shock index (HR/SBP) > 1, ongoing transfusion need ≥ 4 U PRBC in 1 h, or clinical gestalt of massive hemorrhage.
  • Activate MHP by calling blood bank with patient location & identifier; designate a dedicated communication lead and scribe to track products and drugs.
  • Draw baseline labs: CBC, coagulation (INR/PTT, fibrinogen — Clauss method), electrolytes, ionized calcium, creatinine, VBG with lactate and base deficit, type & crossmatch, beta-hCG; consider viscoelastic testing (ROTEM / TEG) where available.

2. Hemorrhage Control & Access

  • Compressible external hemorrhage: direct pressure, wound packing with hemostatic gauze (QuikClot / Combat Gauze), and tactical tourniquet 5–7 cm proximal to wound for extremity arterial bleeding (mark application time).
  • Pelvic binder centred over greater trochanters for suspected pelvic fracture.
  • Junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck): wound packing ± junctional tourniquet (SAM / CRoC).
  • Non-compressible torso hemorrhage: consider REBOA (Zone I or III) at trained trauma centres; activate surgical / interventional radiology teams.
  • Establish 2 large-bore IVs (14–16 G antecubital); IO access (humeral > proximal tibial > sternal) if peripheral access fails within 90 s; central access (rapid-infusion catheter) when available.

3. Resuscitation Strategy (Damage Control)

  • Permissive hypotension: Target SBP 80–90 mmHg or palpable radial pulse until hemorrhage controlled (preserve clot; avoid precipitating rebleed). Exceptions: suspected TBI → SBP > 110 mmHg (Brain Trauma Foundation / Canadian Head Injury); older patients → target SBP 100–110 mmHg.
  • Limit crystalloid to < 1 L: prevents dilutional coagulopathy, acidosis, hypothermia, endotheliopathy. Use balanced crystalloid (Ringer's lactate or Plasma-Lyte) over 0.9% saline.
  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): 1 g IV in 100 mL NS over 10 min as soon as possible and within 3 h of injury (CRASH-2), followed by 1 g IV over 8 h. In TBI with GCS 9–12, give < 2 h of injury (CRASH-3). Also 1 g IV in PPH within 3 h (WOMAN).
  • Recognize and treat the lethal triad: hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy (plus hypocalcemia — "diamond of death").

4. Massive Hemorrhage Protocol (NAC / CBS 2022 Framework)

  • Initial pack (Pack 1): 4 U PRBC (O-negative for females < 45 y; O-positive acceptable otherwise) + 4 U frozen plasma (A liquid plasma or equivalent). Issue 1 adult dose of platelets after each round as supply permits.
  • Subsequent packs: Continue 1:1:1 ratio (PRBC : plasma : platelets). Target fibrinogen ≥ 1.5–2 g/L with fibrinogen concentrate (Fibryga / RiaSTAP) 4 g IV or cryoprecipitate 10 U.
  • Whole blood (low-titre group O): Where available (some Canadian centres via CBS pilot), administer preferentially over components in trauma.
  • Laboratory-guided adjustments (ROTEM/TEG):
    • Low FIBTEM A10 < 7 mm → fibrinogen concentrate 4 g or cryo 10 U.
    • Prolonged EXTEM CT → plasma or 4-factor PCC (Octaplex 25–50 IU/kg).
    • Low platelets on EXTEM A10 < 40 mm → 1 adult dose platelets.
    • Hyperfibrinolysis (ML > 15%) → additional TXA.
  • Calcium replacement: 1 g Calcium gluconate IV (or 1 g Calcium chloride via central line) after every 4 units of blood products, or for ionized calcium < 1.1 mmol/L. Citrate in banked blood chelates calcium; hypocalcemia worsens coagulopathy, myocardial contractility, and vasopressor response.
  • Potassium monitoring: Large-volume transfusion may cause hyperkalemia; if present, treat per standard (Calcium, Insulin + Dextrose, Sodium bicarbonate).
  • Vasopressor as rescue only (do not substitute for blood): low-dose norepinephrine or Vasopressin for MAP support; excess vasopressors worsen end-organ perfusion.

5. Hypothermia Prevention & Environment

  • Remove wet clothing; warm trauma bay to ≥ 25 °C (77 °F).
  • In-line blood/fluid warmer (Level-1, Belmont, Ranger) for all infusions.
  • Forced-air warmer (Bair Hugger), reflective blanket, and warm humidified ventilator gas.
  • Target core temperature > 36 °C.

6. Reversal of Antithrombotics (if contributing)

  • Warfarin: Vitamin K 10 mg IV + 4-factor PCC (Octaplex / Beriplex) 25–50 IU/kg IV per INR.
  • Dabigatran: idarucizumab (Praxbind) 5 g IV.
  • Factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban): andexanet alfa where available, otherwise 4-factor PCC 25–50 IU/kg.
  • Unfractionated heparin: Protamine sulfate 1 mg per 100 U heparin given in the last 2–3 h (max 50 mg).
  • Antiplatelets: Platelet transfusion for life-threatening bleeding or pre-neurosurgery (limited evidence — avoid routine use; PATCH-Trauma suggests harm in spontaneous ICH).

7. Termination / De-Escalation & Audit

  • Stop MHP when hemorrhage controlled, hemodynamics stable, lactate clearing, and ongoing transfusion not required.
  • Serial lactate / base deficit and ionized calcium Q30 min until normalized.
  • Admit ICU for ongoing monitoring of acidosis, coagulopathy, AKI, and abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Complete NAC quality metrics: time to first RBC (< 15 min), plasma:RBC ratio ≥ 1:2, fibrinogen replacement timeliness, and 24-h mortality.
  • Debrief team; reconcile unused blood products with blood bank.
